## 1. Federal Reserve

The Federal Reserve System was born out of a secret meeting on Jekyll Island, Georgia, in November 1910, where six men -- Senator Nelson Aldrich, Assistant Secretary of the Treasury A. Piatt Andrew, and four senior bankers (Henry Davison of J.P. Morgan, Frank Vanderlip of National City Bank, Paul Warburg of Kuhn, Loeb & Co., and Arthur Shelton) -- gathered under cover of a "duck hunting trip" to draft a plan for a central banking system. The participants kept the meeting secret for decades, not admitting it occurred publicly until the 1930s, because they feared that public knowledge of Wall Street's involvement would doom the legislation politically. The resulting plan evolved into the Federal Reserve Act, signed into law by President Woodrow Wilson on December 23, 1913, creating a system of twelve regional reserve banks overseen by a Federal Reserve Board of presidential appointees -- a compromise between bankers who wanted a privately controlled central bank and progressives like William Jennings Bryan who demanded public oversight. The Fed's creation was a response to repeated financial panics throughout the 19th century, most acutely the devastating Panic of 1907, which had demonstrated the need for a lender of last resort. Criticism of the Fed spans the entire political spectrum: left-wing critics accuse it of serving Wall Street interests and being too lenient on large financial institutions, while right-wing and libertarian critics see it as an unconstitutional concentration of monetary power that enables inflation and economic distortion. The Austrian School of economics, represented by figures like Ludwig von Mises, Murray Rothbard, and Friedrich Hayek, offers the most systematic intellectual critique, arguing that the Fed's ability to expand the money supply through credit creation causes artificial booms followed by inevitable busts (the Austrian Business Cycle Theory), and that centralized monetary planning is inherently inferior to a free-market monetary system. G. Edward Griffin's 1994 book *The Creature from Jekyll Island* popularized many of these critiques in conspiratorial terms, framing the Fed as a cartel designed by and for the banking elite. While the Fed's institutional design does involve genuine tensions between public accountability and banker influence -- regional Fed presidents are chosen with input from private banks -- the more extreme conspiracy claims that it is a wholly private institution controlled by shadowy families lack evidentiary support.

## 2. Rothschilds

The Rothschild family is a real and historically significant Ashkenazi Jewish banking dynasty originating from Frankfurt, Germany, whose rise began with Mayer Amschel Rothschild (1744-1812), a court factor to the German Landgraves of Hesse-Kassel who strategically placed each of his five sons in major European financial capitals -- London, Paris, Frankfurt, Vienna, and Naples -- creating the first truly international banking network by the 1820s. The family wielded genuine and enormous financial influence throughout the 19th century: they financed wars, underwrote government bonds, funded the Suez Canal purchase for the British government, and helped pay French war indemnities after the Franco-Prussian War of 1870-1871, making them among the wealthiest families in modern history. However, their real power has been vastly exaggerated by conspiracy theorists for over 180 years, beginning with a fraudulent 1846 French pamphlet titled *Histoire edifante et curieuse de Rothschild Ier, roi des juifs* ("Edifying and Curious History of Rothschild I, King of the Jews"), written under the pseudonym "Satan," which falsely claimed that Nathan Rothschild had personally witnessed the Battle of Waterloo in 1815 and rushed back to London to make a fortune on the stock exchange before news of Napoleon's defeat broke -- a story that is entirely fabricated, as Nathan was not at Waterloo or even in Belgium. These conspiracy theories are recognized by historians, the Anti-Defamation League, and the American Jewish Committee as rooted in antisemitic tropes that portray Jews as secretive puppet-masters controlling global finance, and the word "Rothschild" itself has become a coded antisemitic shorthand for "greedy, manipulative Jewish billionaires." In reality, the Rothschild family's wealth and influence declined significantly in the 20th century relative to the global economy; by 2019, only one family member appeared on the Forbes billionaires list, ranked at number 1,349 -- a far cry from claims of controlling the world's entire financial system. The conspiracy theory that the Rothschilds own or control the Federal Reserve, most of the world's central banks, or orchestrate wars for profit has been repeatedly debunked by fact-checkers including PolitiFact, which found no evidence supporting these claims. The persistence of Rothschild conspiracy theories serves as one of the clearest examples of how real historical wealth and influence can be distorted through the lens of bigotry into unfalsifiable claims of omnipotent secret control.

## 3. Bilderberg Group

The Bilderberg Meeting is an annual, invitation-only conference of approximately 120-150 political leaders, business executives, academics, and media figures from North America and Western Europe, first held from May 29-31, 1954, at the Hotel de Bilderberg in Oosterbeek, Netherlands, from which the group takes its name. It was co-founded by Polish political advisor Jozef Retinger, who was alarmed by rising anti-American sentiment in postwar Western Europe, together with Prince Bernhard of the Netherlands (who provided royal patronage) and with support from American banker David Rockefeller; its original purpose was to strengthen transatlantic relations during the Cold War and contain Soviet influence. The meetings operate under the Chatham House Rule: participants may use information discussed but may not attribute statements to specific individuals, and no official minutes, votes, or policy statements are produced, which has made the proceedings a magnet for conspiracy theories. Past attendees have included heads of state, prime ministers, secretaries of state like Henry Kissinger, and business leaders, as well as figures like Bill Clinton (before his presidency) and David Cameron (before becoming UK Prime Minister), leading some to claim the group acts as a "kingmaker." Prince Bernhard himself resigned as chairman in 1976 after the Lockheed bribery scandal, in which he was accused of accepting $1.1 million in bribes to influence Dutch defense contracts, which forced the cancellation of that year's meeting. Conspiracy theories about Bilderberg span the political spectrum: left-wing critics accuse it of propping up capitalist domination and corporate power, while right-wing critics claim it is laying the groundwork for a one-world government; prominent conspiracy promoters include Alex Jones, the John Birch Society, Phyllis Schlafly, journalist Jim Tucker, and former Minnesota Governor Jesse Ventura. A 2025 Georgetown University analysis concluded that while the meetings do facilitate elite networking and career advancement -- with attendance often correlating with later appointment to high office -- the available evidence shows the event functions as a forum for private discussion rather than a venue for binding treaty-making or formal decision-taking. The group has become somewhat more transparent in recent years, publishing participant lists and broad agenda topics on its official website, though the substance of discussions remains confidential.

## 4. Council on Foreign Relations

The Council on Foreign Relations (CFR) is an independent, nonpartisan American think tank and membership organization focused on U.S. foreign policy and international relations, officially founded on July 29, 1921, with its first honorary president being Elihu Root, a former Secretary of State under Theodore Roosevelt, and its first elected president being corporate lawyer John W. Davis. The founding meetings were attended by 108 high-ranking officers of banking, manufacturing, trading, and finance companies, along with prominent lawyers, establishing from the outset a membership base drawn from the American establishment elite. In 1922, the CFR launched its flagship publication, *Foreign Affairs*, under Professor Archibald Cary Coolidge of Harvard, which became and remains one of the most influential foreign policy journals in the world. Over its century of existence, the CFR's roughly 5,000 members have included senior politicians, multiple Secretaries of State, CIA directors, Supreme Court justices, major media figures, corporate CEOs, and prominent academics, making it an extraordinarily well-connected institution. The "shadow government" conspiracy theory surrounding the CFR was notably articulated by Mary M. Davison in her 1966 booklet *The Profound Revolution*, which claimed that international bankers who created the Federal Reserve in 1913 subsequently formed the CFR in 1921 as a vehicle for covert control of American policy, and later by James Perloff in *The Shadows of Power* (1988), which argued the CFR systematically steered America toward interventionism and globalism against the national interest. Critics from both the populist right and the anti-establishment left point to the revolving door between CFR membership and senior government positions as evidence that the organization shapes policy beyond democratic accountability. The CFR itself describes its mission as providing analysis, convening discussions, and developing policy recommendations, and its publications and events are largely public; however, like many elite institutions, it does host off-the-record discussions under rules similar to those at Bilderberg. While the CFR undeniably represents a concentration of elite influence on foreign policy -- its members have occupied key positions in virtually every presidential administration since its founding -- the leap from "influential think tank with elite membership" to "secret world government" requires evidence that conspiracy theorists have not provided.

## 5. Bohemian Grove

The Bohemian Grove is a 2,700-acre private campground in Monte Rio, California, owned by the Bohemian Club, an elite all-male social club founded in 1872 in San Francisco originally as an intellectual retreat for artists, journalists, and writers -- including early members like Mark Twain, Jack London, and Ambrose Bierce -- that over the decades evolved into one of the most exclusive clubs in the United States, with approximately 2,600 members drawn from the highest ranks of politics, business, and the arts, with a waiting list that can stretch 15 years. Every July, the Grove hosts a two-and-a-half-week encampment where members and invited guests gather in rustic camps among ancient redwood trees; historically, attendees have included Presidents Roosevelt, Taft, Hoover, Eisenhower, Nixon, Ford, Reagan, and both Bushes, as well as figures like Henry Kissinger, Walter Cronkite, and Clint Eastwood. The most famous event at the Grove is the Cremation of Care, a theatrical ceremony first performed in 1881, in which robed members carry out a symbolic ritual at the foot of a 40-foot concrete owl, burning an effigy called "Dull Care" to represent the casting off of worldly worries for the duration of the retreat -- the club describes it as a lighthearted, communal catharsis meant to promote fellowship and creative openness. The Grove also played a role in real history: in September 1942, a meeting there involving Ernest Lawrence, J. Robert Oppenheimer, and members of the S-1 Executive Committee was related to early planning discussions of the Manhattan Project, and in 1950, Dwight Eisenhower and Richard Nixon first met at the Grove as guests of former President Herbert Hoover. On July 15, 2000, radio host Alex Jones and cameraman Mike Hanson secretly entered the Grove and filmed the Cremation of Care ceremony with a hidden camera, footage that became the documentary *Dark Secrets: Inside Bohemian Grove*; Jones characterized the ceremony as a "ritual sacrifice" involving occult Luciferian practices, a characterization not supported by other reporting, which has described the same footage as a theatrical, symbolic ritual and an "overgrown private frat party." While the extreme secrecy and exclusivity of the Grove -- combined with its genuinely powerful membership roster -- make it a legitimate subject of concern about elite networking outside democratic scrutiny, the claims of satanic worship and human sacrifice that circulate in conspiracy communities are unsupported by evidence.

## 6. Skull and Bones

Skull and Bones is a secret society at Yale University, founded in 1832 by William Huntington Russell and Alphonso Taft (father of future President William Howard Taft), reportedly modeled after secret societies Russell encountered while studying in Germany; the society was originally called the Eulogian Club and was incorporated in 1856 as the Russell Trust Association, which manages its finances and properties. Each spring, the society "taps" (selects) exactly 15 rising seniors from the junior class in a ceremony known as "tap day," and its membership roster over nearly two centuries reads as a who's who of American power: three U.S. Presidents who were Yale undergraduates (William Howard Taft, George H.W. Bush, and George W. Bush), as well as Secretary of State and 2004 presidential candidate John Kerry, magazine founder Henry Luce, conservative intellectual William F. Buckley Jr., diplomat Averell Harriman, historian David McCullough, and Prescott Bush (father and grandfather of the presidential Bushes). The society's headquarters is a windowless building on Yale's campus known as "The Tomb," and members reportedly refer to outsiders as "barbarians"; despite the society's secrecy, its existence has been publicly known since the 19th century and has been covered extensively by journalists and authors. The 2004 presidential election drew particular attention to Skull and Bones because both major-party nominees -- George W. Bush and John Kerry -- were members, and when asked about this on *Meet the Press*, Kerry replied dismissively, "Not much, because it's a secret." The society did not admit women until 1992, despite Yale having gone coeducational in 1969, and its membership has drawn from families including the Rockefellers, Vanderbilts, Fords, and Whitneys, reinforcing perceptions of it as a pipeline for the American establishment. Conspiracy theories about Skull and Bones range from claims that it is a branch of the Bavarian Illuminati to allegations that it functions as a recruitment vehicle for the CIA or a shadow network that controls American politics; while the society does represent a genuine elite networking mechanism and its members have held extraordinary concentrations of power, the more extreme claims of a coordinated conspiracy to control the government remain unsubstantiated. The secrecy itself -- including initiation rituals that reportedly involve lying in a coffin and sharing personal confessions -- feeds speculation, but leaked internal documents and investigative reporting suggest the society functions primarily as an intense bonding experience and lifelong networking fraternity rather than a sinister command structure.

## 7. Fiat Currency

Fiat currency -- money that derives its value from government decree rather than intrinsic value or backing by a physical commodity like gold -- became the dominant global monetary system on August 15, 1971, when President Richard Nixon announced what became known as the "Nixon Shock": the unilateral cancellation of the direct international convertibility of the U.S. dollar to gold at the fixed rate of $35 per ounce, effectively ending the Bretton Woods system that had governed international monetary relations since 1944. The Bretton Woods system, established at the end of World War II, had pegged all major currencies to the dollar, which itself was convertible to gold, creating a system of fixed exchange rates; by the late 1960s, however, a growing surplus of dollars overseas driven by foreign aid, military spending (especially in Vietnam), and foreign investment meant the U.S. no longer had enough gold reserves to honor its convertibility promise, creating a looming crisis. Nixon intended the suspension to be temporary while reforms were negotiated, but all attempts at reform failed, and by 1973 the world had transitioned to the system of floating fiat currencies that persists today. The Nixon Shock has been described as a political success but an economic mixed bag: it contributed to the 1973-1975 recession, the stagflation of the 1970s, and the instability inherent in floating exchange rates, while also giving governments and central banks the flexibility to respond to economic crises without the constraints of gold backing. Since 1971, the U.S. money supply has expanded enormously -- currency in circulation grew from $48.6 billion in 1971 to over $5.2 trillion by 2020 -- and critics point to this as evidence that fiat money inevitably leads to debasement, inflation, and the erosion of purchasing power. The gold standard debate remains active: proponents argue that gold backing imposes fiscal discipline on governments, prevents excessive money printing, and protects the purchasing power of savings, while opponents contend that a gold standard is too rigid, limits the ability to respond to economic downturns, and that the historical gold standard era was actually marked by frequent deflation, bank panics, and deep recessions. Austrian economists like Mises and Rothbard are among the most vocal critics of fiat money, arguing that it enables the state and banking system to create money from nothing, redistributing wealth through inflation from savers to debtors and from ordinary people to those closest to the money spigot (the "Cantillon effect"). The website WTFHappenedin1971.com, popular in cryptocurrency and hard-money communities, compiles charts showing divergences in wages, productivity, inequality, and other metrics that began around 1971, arguing that the end of gold convertibility was a pivotal inflection point in economic history.

## 8. Babylonian Money Magic

"Babylonian Money Magic" is a term used primarily in conspiracy and alternative-history communities to describe the modern fractional reserve banking system, drawing an analogy to ancient Mesopotamian financial practices and framing contemporary debt-based money creation as a form of occult deception perpetrated on an unwitting public. The core factual kernel is that fractional reserve banking does involve a form of money creation: when a bank makes a loan, it does not lend out pre-existing money from its vault but rather creates new money by crediting the borrower's account, keeping only a fraction (historically around 10%) of deposits in reserve -- a process that, through the money multiplier effect, allows the banking system to create significantly more money than the central bank's original base money. This is not a secret or conspiracy; it is described in standard economics textbooks and has been openly acknowledged by central banks, including the Bank of England, which published a 2014 paper titled "Money Creation in the Modern Economy" explaining exactly how commercial banks create money through lending. The conspiracy narrative, however, frames this process as a deliberate, ancient scheme -- tracing it to Babylonian merchants around 2000 BC who practiced early forms of lending at interest -- arguing that a priestly class discovered that receipts for stored grain or gold could circulate as money even when there was not enough grain or gold to honor all the receipts, and that this "magical" trick of creating something from nothing has been passed down through secret societies to modern central bankers. Critics of fractional reserve banking include serious economists as well as conspiracy theorists: the Austrian School argues it is inherently fraudulent and destabilizing because it creates fiduciary media (money substitutes not backed by real savings), while post-Keynesian economists like Steve Keen and proponents of Modern Monetary Theory acknowledge the debt-based nature of money creation but draw very different policy conclusions. The term "money magic" is meant to evoke the idea that the public is under a spell, unaware that the money in their bank accounts is largely created through debt and could theoretically vanish in a bank run, but mainstream economists counter that this system, while imperfect, has enabled economic growth and flexibility that commodity-backed money could not provide. The conspiratorial framing -- connecting ancient Babylon to the Knights Templar to the Rothschilds to the Federal Reserve in an unbroken chain of secret monetary manipulation -- lacks historical evidence for the claimed continuity and is rejected by economic historians.

## 9. Maritime/Admiralty Law

The maritime or admiralty law theory is a central doctrine of the sovereign citizen movement, a loose network of individuals in the United States (with parallel movements in the UK, Canada, and Australia) who believe that by exploiting supposed legal loopholes, they can exempt themselves from the jurisdiction of courts, taxation, and government authority. The core claim is that American courts are secretly operating under admiralty law -- the body of law governing maritime commerce and navigation -- rather than common law, and that this means they have no legitimate jurisdiction over flesh-and-blood human beings unless those individuals consent to that jurisdiction; supposed evidence for this includes the presence of gold-fringed flags in courtrooms (which sovereign citizens claim signal admiralty jurisdiction) and the use of legal terminology they trace to maritime commerce. The theory is intertwined with the "strawman" doctrine, which holds that each person has two identities: a flesh-and-blood natural person and a fictional legal entity (the "strawman") created by the government at birth, represented by one's name in ALL CAPITAL LETTERS on official documents; sovereign citizens believe that the government's authority extends only to this fictional entity and that by filing certain paperwork, one can "separate" from the strawman and escape all legal obligations. These ideas emerged primarily in the 1990s and early 2000s, with the strawman concept specifically attributed to North Dakota farmer Roger Elvick, who developed the "redemption" scheme around 1999-2000 and was later convicted of fraud. Courts at every level -- federal, state, and in multiple countries -- have uniformly and repeatedly rejected these arguments as frivolous, nonsensical, and legally baseless; as one federal judge stated, "Regardless of an individual's claimed status of descent, be it as a 'sovereign citizen,' a 'secured-party creditor,' or a 'flesh-and-blood human being,' that person is not beyond the jurisdiction of the courts." The FBI classifies sovereign citizen extremists as a domestic terrorism threat, not because of their beliefs per se, but because some adherents have responded violently when their legal theories fail in court, including attacks on police officers and judges. The IRS specifically lists sovereign citizen tax arguments as frivolous positions that can result in a $5,000 penalty, and promoters of "redemption" schemes have been prosecuted for fraud.

## 10. Birth Certificate / SSN

The sovereign citizen theories around birth certificates and Social Security Numbers represent an extension of the strawman doctrine (discussed above), holding that when a child is born in the United States and a birth certificate is issued, the government secretly creates a corporate entity -- a "strawman" -- in the child's name, represented by the name printed in ALL CAPITAL LETTERS on official documents, and simultaneously opens a secret Treasury Department account funded with somewhere between $600,000 and $20 million (the exact figure varies by which sovereign citizen you ask). Proponents claim that each American's birth certificate is actually a bond or security that is traded on international markets, and that the Social Security Number functions as a stock or bond tracking number for this secret account; they argue that by filing specific documents -- often called "UCC (Uniform Commercial Code) filings" -- one can access this secret account to pay off debts, discharge taxes, or even draw funds. The theory rests on the claim that the United States government secretly went bankrupt in 1933 (during the New Deal and the abandonment of the domestic gold standard) and pledged its citizens as collateral to international bankers, effectively turning every American into corporate property. In practice, people who attempt to use these theories file fraudulent documents, write worthless "bonds" drawn on their supposed Treasury accounts, or present bizarre legal arguments in court; the results are invariably the same -- the documents are rejected, the legal arguments are dismissed, and the individuals are often prosecuted for fraud, filing false liens, or tax evasion. PolitiFact has explicitly debunked the claim that "your legal name is a corporation," calling it based on a discredited conspiracy theory with no basis in law. The IRS treats these claims as frivolous tax positions subject to penalties, and the Department of Justice has successfully prosecuted numerous promoters of these schemes, including Roger Elvick himself, for fraud. While the theories dress themselves in the language of liberty and resistance to government overreach, they have caused real financial harm to the people who follow them, who often end up with criminal records, liens on their property, and heavy fines.

## 11. Committee of 300

The "Committee of 300" is a conspiracy theory popularized primarily by John Coleman, who claimed to be a former MI-6 British intelligence agent, in his 1992 book *Conspirators' Hierarchy: The Story of the Committee of 300*. Coleman's central thesis is that a shadowy committee of 300 individuals -- drawn from the world's oldest and wealthiest families, royal houses, and banking dynasties -- secretly controls every government, every university, every charitable organization, every bank, and every media outlet on Earth, either directly or through intermediary organizations like the Bilderberg Group, the CFR, the Club of Rome, and the Trilateral Commission. According to Coleman, the Committee's goals include reducing the world population through forced abortions and sterilizations, promoting drug addiction to pacify and control the masses, using entertainment and media to dismantle traditional family structures, and ultimately establishing a totalitarian "One World Government" under their control. The book has been widely criticized by scholars and reviewers for its near-total lack of verifiable sourcing -- Coleman repeatedly references "secret documents" and "insider knowledge" but provides almost no citations that can be independently checked, making his claims unfalsifiable by design. Despite its lack of scholarly credibility, the book has had significant cultural influence in conspiracy circles; it was found in the Abbottabad compound where Osama bin Laden was killed in 2011 (a CIA-hosted PDF of the book from the compound is publicly available), and its ideas have been adopted and expanded by conspiracy figures like David Icke and Alex Jones. The concept appears to draw on older conspiracy traditions, including Nesta Webster's interwar writings about secret societies and the long history of claims about Illuminati control; the number "300" may reference Walter Rathenau's 1909 statement (often quoted out of context) that "three hundred men, all of whom know one another, guide the economic destinies of the Continent." No credible evidence has ever been presented for the existence of the Committee of 300 as a coordinated governing body, and the theory is classified by researchers as a conspiratorial framework that weaves real organizations and individuals into an unfalsifiable grand narrative.

## 12. Georgia Guidestones

The Georgia Guidestones were a granite monument erected in 1980 in Elbert County, Georgia, commissioned in 1979 by a man using the pseudonym "R.C. Christian" -- who told the Elberton Granite Finishing Company he represented "a small group of loyal Americans" but insisted on absolute anonymity -- and sometimes called "America's Stonehenge" for their resemblance to the ancient British monument. The structure consisted of four 19-foot-tall granite slabs arranged around a central pillar, with a capstone on top, and served astronomical functions as a sundial and celestial calendar; sandblasted into the slabs were over 4,000 characters in eight languages (English, Spanish, Swahili, Hindi, Hebrew, Arabic, Traditional Chinese, and Russian) setting out ten guidelines for humanity. The most controversial of these guidelines was the first: "Maintain humanity under 500,000,000 in perpetual balance with nature," which, given that the world population was already over 4 billion when the stones were erected, was interpreted by critics as advocating mass depopulation; other guidelines included calls for a world court, guiding reproduction wisely, and ruling with "tempered reason." The true identity of R.C. Christian was a mystery for decades, but a 2024 CNN investigation by journalist Thomas Lake identified him as Dr. Herbert Hinzie Kersten, an Iowa physician who died in 2005, based on information inadvertently revealed by an Elberton banker on camera during a documentary; Kersten had written a book advocating population control measures including policies similar to China's one-child policy. The monument attracted conspiracy theories from its unveiling, with some viewing it as the "commandments" of a New World Order elite, a Satanic or Rosicrucian monument, or a blueprint for post-apocalyptic global governance; far-right politician and Georgia gubernatorial candidate Kandiss Taylor campaigned in 2022 on a promise to demolish what she called "Satanic" stones. In the early morning hours of July 6, 2022, a bomb severely damaged the monument, and local authorities demolished the remaining structure later that day for safety reasons; no suspects have been publicly identified, and the case remains unsolved, with a 2026 podcast by the Atlanta Journal-Constitution continuing to investigate the bombing. The monument's destruction ended a 42-year run as one of America's most unusual and debated public landmarks.

## 13. New World Order

The phrase "New World Order" has a long history in mainstream political discourse, used by leaders including Woodrow Wilson and Winston Churchill to describe the reorganization of international power after major wars, but it became a lightning rod for conspiracy theorists after President George H.W. Bush used it prominently in his September 11, 1990, address to a joint session of Congress, in which he described "a new world order, where diverse nations are drawn together in common cause to achieve the universal aspirations of mankind -- peace and security, freedom, and the rule of law." Bush used the phrase at least 42 times between the summer of 1990 and March 1991, along with Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev, to describe their vision for post-Cold War international cooperation, and it was intended as an optimistic description of multilateral diplomacy after the fall of the Berlin Wall and during the Gulf War coalition-building. However, the phrase was almost immediately reappropriated by conspiracy theorists, who interpreted it as an admission of a long-standing plan by global elites to establish a single authoritarian world government that would abolish national sovereignty, disarm citizens, and impose totalitarian control. The conspiracy theory's modern genealogy can be traced through several key vectors: Pat Robertson's 1991 bestseller *The New World Order*, which argued that Wall Street, the Federal Reserve, the CFR, the Bilderberg Group, and the Trilateral Commission were secretly steering the world toward an Antichrist-led global government; the John Birch Society, which had promoted similar ideas since the 1950s; and militia movement figures like Linda Thompson and Mark Koernke in the 1990s, whose rhetoric contributed to the rise of armed anti-government groups. The concept draws on much older conspiratorial traditions, including fears of Illuminati control dating to the late 18th century, anti-Masonic panics, and antisemitic theories about Jewish world domination (particularly the fraudulent *Protocols of the Elders of Zion*). Scholars at the Middlebury Institute have documented how the NWO conspiracy functions as a "super-conspiracy" -- a meta-narrative that absorbs and connects disparate conspiracy theories (about the UN, vaccines, gun control, 9/11, etc.) into a single overarching framework, making it one of the most durable and adaptable conspiracy templates of the modern era. In the 2020s, the NWO framework has been updated to incorporate the COVID-19 pandemic, the World Economic Forum's Great Reset, and climate change policies as alleged components of the plan.

## 14. Agenda 21 / Agenda 2030

Agenda 21 is a non-binding action plan of the United Nations adopted at the 1992 Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, addressing sustainable development, combating poverty, protecting health, and preventing deforestation; it is a 351-page document organized into 40 chapters covering issues like land management, biodiversity, and capacity-building in developing countries, and is explicitly non-binding -- meaning it imposes no legal obligations on any nation. Its successor, the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, was adopted by all 193 UN member states in September 2015, establishing 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) including ending poverty and hunger, ensuring quality education, achieving gender equality, promoting clean energy, and combating climate change -- again, as a voluntary framework with no enforcement mechanism. The conspiracy theory interpretation, which gained significant traction in American conservative and libertarian circles beginning around 2012, reframes these documents as a blueprint for global totalitarian control: claims include that Agenda 21/2030 will abolish private property rights, force rural populations into dense "smart cities" they cannot leave, ban private car ownership, mandate insect consumption instead of meat, forcibly depopulate the planet, and establish a one-world government. These claims have been promoted by figures including Glenn Beck (who wrote a dystopian novel titled *Agenda 21*), Alex Jones, and various Tea Party organizations, and have led to concrete political action: several U.S. states and counties passed resolutions opposing Agenda 21, and the Republican National Committee adopted a resolution in 2012 opposing it as "destructive and insidious." Multiple fact-checking organizations -- including PolitiFact, Snopes, Full Fact, and The Journal -- have examined these claims and found them to be distortions or fabrications: the actual UN documents contain no references to abolishing private property, banning cars, mandating insect diets, or establishing world government, and the frequently shared viral "Agenda 21/2030" lists circulating on social media are fabrications not found in any UN document. The conflation of "Agenda 21" and "Agenda 2030" into a single sinister plan (often written as "Agenda 21/2030") is itself misleading, as these are two separate documents adopted 23 years apart; while they share themes of sustainability, neither contains the totalitarian provisions attributed to them by conspiracy theorists.

## 15. World Economic Forum / Great Reset

The World Economic Forum (WEF) was founded in 1971 by German engineer and economist Klaus Schwab as the "European Management Forum," initially a conference for European business leaders to learn about American management practices, held annually in Davos, Switzerland; it was renamed the World Economic Forum in 1987 as its scope expanded to encompass global economic, political, and social issues, and it introduced a membership system for what it described as the world's 1,000 leading companies. Schwab's concept of "stakeholder capitalism" -- the idea that companies should serve not just shareholders but all stakeholders including employees, communities, and the environment -- has been a central WEF theme since its founding, and in 2016 Schwab published *The Fourth Industrial Revolution*, arguing that emerging technologies like artificial intelligence, robotics, and biotechnology would fundamentally transform economies and societies in ways requiring coordinated global governance. The "Great Reset Initiative" was launched by the WEF in June 2020, in the early months of the COVID-19 pandemic, with Schwab and Prince Charles proposing three core components: steering markets toward fairer outcomes through updated regulations and tax policies, investing pandemic recovery funds in sustainable and equitable development, and harnessing Fourth Industrial Revolution technologies for the public good. The conspiracy theory version of the Great Reset, which spread rapidly through social media during 2020-2021, claims that global elites orchestrated or exploited the COVID-19 pandemic to implement a plan to abolish private property, impose authoritarian control, eliminate cash, forcibly vaccinate populations, and restructure society so that ordinary people will "own nothing and be happy." The phrase "You'll own nothing and you'll be happy" originated from a 2016 essay by Danish politician Ida Auken published on the WEF website, which described a speculative future city of shared services; it was never an official WEF policy proposal, and the WEF has explicitly stated it has no goal of eliminating private property. The conspiracy theory was amplified by media figures including Tucker Carlson, Glenn Beck, Candace Owens, and Russell Brand, and spread through platforms like 4chan, Fox News, and Sky News Australia, becoming a rallying cry for populist movements worldwide. Legitimate criticisms of the WEF do exist from both left and right: progressives criticize it as a venue for corporate elites to capture the sustainability agenda and expand their influence over policymaking while avoiding meaningful regulation, while conservatives object to its promotion of ESG metrics and its perceived technocratic overreach; the openDemocracy analysis noted that "there is something fishy about the Great Reset" even setting conspiracy theories aside, pointing to the gap between Schwab's reformist rhetoric and the WEF's track record of serving corporate interests. Under Schwab's leadership until his transition to a governance role, the WEF grew into a global institution with offices in Geneva, New York, San Francisco, Beijing, and Tokyo, wielding significant convening power even though it has no formal governmental authority.
