## 1. Antigravity -- History of Research, Thomas Townsend Brown, Podkletnov, Boeing

The history of antigravity research stretches back to the 1920s, when American inventor Thomas Townsend Brown (1905--1985) noticed that a high-voltage Coolidge X-ray tube seemed to change mass depending on its orientation on a balance scale. Brown spent the following decade developing devices he called "gravitators" -- essentially large asymmetric capacitors that combined high voltages with materials of high dielectric constants -- and named the observed phenomenon the "Biefeld-Brown effect" after himself and his mentor, physicist Paul Alfred Biefeld. In 1930, Brown pitched the gravitator as propulsion technology to General Motors executives, and in 1932 he proposed it for ship propulsion while working at the Naval Research Laboratory, also participating in a Navy-Princeton gravity expedition aboard submarine S-48. Throughout the 1950s, major aerospace companies including Boeing, Bell, Convair, Douglas, Glenn Martin, General Electric, Lear, and Sperry-Rand reportedly explored electrogravitics research, and concurrent programs ran in Britain, France, Sweden, Canada, and Germany between 1954 and 1956. In 1992, Russian researcher Eugene Podkletnov claimed that a rapidly rotating superconducting ceramic disc could reduce the weight of objects placed above it by up to 2%, but every subsequent attempt to replicate the experiment -- including efforts by NASA (which spent $650,000 on apparatus that could not achieve the required spin rate), the University of Sheffield (funded by the UK Ministry of Defence and BAE Systems), and the University of Toronto -- produced null results. Boeing was alleged to be funding a project called GRASP (Gravity Research for Advanced Space Propulsion) based on Podkletnov's work, but Boeing denied funding GRASP with company money. The mainstream scientific explanation for Brown's observations is electrohydrodynamics -- ionic wind created by charged particles transferring momentum to surrounding air molecules -- and experiments in vacuum chambers failed to reproduce the alleged gravitational effects.

## 2. Electrogravitics -- 1950s Research, What Happened, Declassification

Electrogravitics refers to the alleged science of using high-voltage electricity to provide propulsive force to aircraft or spacecraft, rooted in Thomas Townsend Brown's experiments from the 1920s onward. During the mid-1950s, the field attracted significant attention from the aerospace-defense establishment: two key 1956 documents -- "Electrogravitics Systems" and "The Gravitics Situation" -- were produced by the Gravity Research Group of London (a Special Weapons Study Unit) and detailed industry-wide involvement from companies including Douglas, Glenn Martin, General Electric, Bell, Convair, Lear, and Sperry-Rand. In 1985, researcher Paul LaViolette obtained the formerly classified 1956 report "Electrogravitics Systems" from Wright-Patterson Air Force Base through a declassification request, revealing that a substantial R&D program had existed during the 1950s aimed at practical field propulsion applications. However, the research trail goes cold after the mid-1950s: open publications on the topic largely disappeared, suggesting the work either concluded without results or was absorbed into classified programs. A critical blow came in September 1952, when the Office of Naval Research evaluated Brown's work and dismissed it as a "well-known phenomenon of the electric wind." Detailed studies conducted in vacuum chambers -- which would eliminate ionic wind as a factor -- failed to replicate Brown's claimed antigravity effects. The scientific consensus today holds that the Biefeld-Brown effect is an electrohydrodynamic phenomenon (ion drift), not a gravitational one, and no peer-reviewed evidence has established a link between electric fields and gravitational modification. Nevertheless, the fact that the research visibly disappeared from the open literature after the 1950s continues to fuel speculation in conspiracy circles about suppression of breakthrough technology.

## 3. Cold Fusion -- Pons and Fleischmann (1989), Subsequent Research, LENR

On March 23, 1989, electrochemists Stanley Pons and Martin Fleischmann of the University of Utah held a press conference announcing they had achieved nuclear fusion at room temperature in a simple tabletop apparatus using palladium electrodes and heavy water (deuterium oxide), reporting anomalous excess heat and small amounts of nuclear byproducts including neutrons and tritium. The announcement triggered a worldwide frenzy of attempted replications, but hopes faded rapidly as the vast majority of labs reported negative results, many initially positive replications were withdrawn, flaws and sources of experimental error in the original setup were identified, and it was discovered that Pons and Fleischmann had not actually detected the nuclear byproducts they claimed. By late 1989, the scientific establishment largely declared cold fusion dead, and the episode became a cautionary tale about premature announcements and the importance of peer review. Pons and Fleischmann never retracted their claims but relocated their research from the U.S. to a Toyota-funded lab in France, where work continued quietly until Fleischmann's retirement. A small but persistent community of researchers continued investigating the phenomenon under rebranded names -- most commonly Low-Energy Nuclear Reactions (LENR) or Condensed Matter Nuclear Science (CMNS). In 2006, physicists Allan Widom and Lewis Larsen published a theory in the European Physical Journal C proposing that the observed effects could be explained by ultra-low-momentum neutron creation and recapture, a mechanism consistent with known physics that has nothing to do with fusion. Google spent approximately $10 million from 2015 onward funding LENR research with teams from MIT, the University of British Columbia, and Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory; their results, published in Nature in 2019, found no evidence of classic cold fusion but did observe anomalies suggestive of localized nuclear reactions in metals. In 2021, the U.S. Naval Surface Warfare Center assembled a cross-service team (Navy, Army, NIST) for a coordinated new study, and in 2025 University of British Columbia researchers published results in Nature showing a modest 15% performance boost using an electrochemical deuterium-loading technique -- the first demonstration of deuterium-deuterium fusion using this method, though still at benchtop scale with more energy input than output.

## 4. Overunity Devices -- Claims, Tests, Why They Violate Thermodynamics

Overunity devices are machines that their proponents claim produce more energy output than the energy input required to operate them, effectively creating energy from nothing and promising a source of "free energy." Such devices directly violate the first law of thermodynamics (conservation of energy), which states that energy cannot be created or destroyed but only transformed from one form to another, as well as the second law of thermodynamics, which dictates that entropy in a closed system always increases, making perpetual motion impossible. The most prominent historical example is Joseph Newman's Energy Machine, a DC motor which Newman claimed in the late 1970s produced mechanical power exceeding its electrical input; his patent application was rejected by the USPTO as a perpetual motion machine, and when the case went to court, the National Bureau of Standards tested the device in 1986 and conclusively measured input power exceeding output power across all operating conditions. Despite the NBS findings, Newman attracted thousands of supporters, packed the New Orleans Superdome for a demonstration, and gained the backing of multiple U.S. senators and congressmen from Mississippi. Another serial promoter, Dennis Lee, learned the overunity business from Newman and since 1988 has promised audiences that they can "disconnect from the power grid and never pay another electric bill" using a Fischer engine, a Counter Rotating Device, and an overunity motor -- none of which have been independently verified. In 2007, the Alabama Securities Commission issued a cease-and-desist order against the Newman Energy Corporation for selling unregistered stock. YouTube and social media have become major vectors for overunity claims, with channels demonstrating magnet motors and "free energy generators" that invariably rely on hidden power sources, measurement errors, or selective editing. No overunity device has ever been demonstrated to work under rigorous, independently controlled scientific conditions, and every claim that has been subjected to proper testing has been found to produce less output than input, consistent with thermodynamic law.

## 5. Suppressed Patents -- The Invention Secrecy Act of 1951 and Secrecy Orders

The Invention Secrecy Act of 1951 (35 U.S.C. ch. 17) is a real United States federal law that authorizes the government to impose "secrecy orders" on patent applications that contain information deemed detrimental to national security if publicly disclosed. The law empowers federal defense and intelligence agencies to review patent applications flagged by the USPTO and compel their classification, withholding the grant of a patent and prohibiting the inventor from disclosing the invention -- with penalties of up to two years in prison and $10,000 in fines for violations. During World War II, before the Act was formally passed, the U.S. Patent Office placed approximately 11,000 patent applications related to the war effort under secrecy in technological areas including radar, electronics, synthetic materials, and cryptography. The USPTO's Group 220 historically conducted secrecy reviews of all applications, using the Armed Services Patent Advisory Board's Patent Security Category Review List, which contained 21 invention categories of security interest to agencies such as the Army, Navy, Air Force, Atomic Energy Commission, NASA, and NSA. At the end of fiscal year 2025, there were 6,543 secrecy orders in effect. In 2015, the USPTO issued 95 new secrecy orders -- roughly one for every 6,600 patent applications filed. Remarkably, secrecy orders can be imposed even on inventions generated and entirely owned by private individuals or companies without any government sponsorship; approximately twelve inventions by private, non-government-affiliated inventors are classified per year on average. Many affected inventions involve dual-use technologies with both civilian and military applications, such as advances in AI, GPS, or drone technology. Inventors subject to secrecy orders may apply for compensation from the government for damages, but the process is slow, and the secrecy orders are renewed annually and can remain in place indefinitely -- some have persisted for decades.

## 6. Med Beds -- Conspiracy Claim About Healing Technology

Med beds (or "medbeds") are a nonexistent medical technology that became prominent in conspiracy theory narratives primarily through QAnon and adjacent wellness/techno-mysticism communities in the early 2020s. Proponents typically describe three types: a holographic diagnostic/curative bed, a regenerative bed that can regrow limbs, and an age-reversing bed -- sometimes attributed to secret military programs, reverse-engineered alien technology, or suppressed advanced science. The concept draws heavily from science fiction, particularly the medical pods seen in films like "Elysium" (2013) and the regeneration tanks in "Star Wars," as well as the broader sci-fi trope of automated healing chambers. The conspiracy theory spread primarily through Telegram, Facebook, and TikTok communities that blend New Age vocabulary -- "frequencies," "biophotons," "quantum," "terahertz," "tachyons" -- with narratives about global elites hoarding transformative technology from ordinary people. Science communicators and fact-checkers, including McGill University's Office for Science and Society, have characterized medbed claims as a "word salad of whatever sounds futuristic," noting that the described mechanisms conflict with established biology and clinical evidence. Despite the lack of any scientific basis, medbed claims have generated real commercial activity: entrepreneurs have opened "medbed centers" charging clients for sessions on light-therapy or PEMF (pulsed electromagnetic field) devices rebranded as "medbeds," exploiting desperate patients. In September 2025, a video promoting medbeds was shared on social media by former President Trump, significantly amplifying the concept's visibility and lending it a veneer of political endorsement. Medical professionals and researchers universally agree that no device exists or is under development that can instantaneously cure diseases, regrow limbs, or reverse aging, and that the promotion of medbeds poses a public health risk by diverting people from evidence-based treatments.

## 7. Food Replicators -- Star Trek Influence and 3D Food Printing Reality

The concept of a food replicator originates from the Star Trek franchise, where the device converts energy directly into matter to produce any food or drink on demand, and appeared as standard equipment aboard Federation starships beginning in Star Trek: The Next Generation (1987). This science fiction concept has been adopted by certain conspiracy communities associated with the "Great Awakening" narrative, who claim that replicator technology already exists but is being suppressed by elites or will be released to the public as part of a coming global transformation alongside other allegedly hidden technologies. In reality, 3D food printing is an active and legitimate area of research and development, with organizations including Columbia University, NASA, and the European Union investing in the technology. Current 3D food printers work by extruding and layering edible pastes, gels, chocolates, and doughs -- they assemble pre-existing ingredients and can apply heat to cook them, but they cannot create food from pure energy or synthesize nutrients from raw atoms as Star Trek's fictional replicators do. NASA has funded research into 3D food printing for long-duration space missions, recognizing the potential for customized nutrition, reduced food waste, and extended shelf life. The technology has already reached some commercial and culinary applications: restaurants have used 3D printers to create intricate chocolate sculptures and custom pasta shapes. However, experts estimate that true Star Trek-style matter replication -- converting energy to matter at the atomic level -- is at least 100 years away, if it is physically possible at all, because the energy requirements for matter-energy conversion are astronomical (as described by E=mc²) and there is currently no way to make complex objects materialize within seconds. The gap between actual 3D food printing (assembling ingredients) and the conspiracy claim (suppressed Star Trek-level replicators) is vast and unbridgeable by any known science.

## 8. Quantum Financial System (QFS) -- NESARA/GESARA Conspiracy

The Quantum Financial System (QFS) is a fictional financial infrastructure that conspiracy theorists claim will replace the entire global banking system using quantum computing technology to provide unhackable security, total transparency, and asset-backed currency. It is closely linked to the NESARA/GESARA conspiracy narrative. NESARA (National Economic Security and Reformation Act) began as a legitimate, if eccentric, policy proposal by Harvey Francis Barnard (1941--2005), a systems theory PhD who during the late 1980s and early 1990s drafted a plan to replace income tax with a national sales tax, abolish compound interest on secured loans, and return to a bimetallic currency; he self-published it as "Draining the Swamp" in 1996 and sent copies to every member of Congress, but the proposals were never introduced. In 2000, Internet personality Shaini Candace Goodwin, using the alias "Dove of Oneness," appropriated NESARA without Barnard's consent and recast it as a secretly enacted law -- the "National Economic Security and Reformation Act" -- that would cancel all personal debts, abolish the IRS, declare world peace, and force new elections, but which was allegedly being suppressed by the Bush administration and the Supreme Court. Barnard publicly condemned Goodwin's version as disinformation before his death in 2005. GESARA (Global Economic Security and Reformation Act) is the international extension of this fantasy, promising worldwide debt forgiveness, a single gold-backed currency, and the end of poverty. The QFS concept was layered on top in the late 2010s, borrowing "quantum" as a buzzword to imply invincibility. None of these -- QFS, NESARA as enacted law, or GESARA -- correspond to any real government program, enacted legislation, or operational financial system. The conspiracy has generated significant financial harm: scammers have used QFS/NESARA/GESARA narratives to sell phishing schemes, fake "activation" devices, worthless crypto tokens, and precious metals, causing real losses through direct fraud and indirect harm such as mortgage defaults by believers who stopped paying bills in anticipation of debt jubilee.

## 9. Black-Shelved Patents -- How Patent Secrecy Actually Works

The term "black-shelved patents" is informal shorthand for patents suppressed under the Invention Secrecy Act of 1951, and the process by which this occurs is well-documented in U.S. law and USPTO procedures. When a patent application is filed with the USPTO, it is first screened by the office's internal reviewers (historically Group 220) against the Armed Services Patent Advisory Board's Patent Security Category Review List -- a document containing 21 categories of inventions of security interest to agencies including the Army, Navy, Air Force, NSA, NASA, and the Department of Energy. If an application is flagged as potentially sensitive, it is forwarded to the relevant defense or intelligence agency for review, and that agency has 90 days to determine whether disclosure of the invention would be detrimental to national security. If the agency so determines, a secrecy order is issued under 35 U.S.C. Section 181, which withholds the patent grant, prohibits the inventor from publicly disclosing the invention or filing for foreign patents, and can impose criminal penalties (up to two years imprisonment and $10,000 fine) for violations. Secrecy orders are reviewed and renewed annually, and some have remained in force for decades; the inventor may petition for rescission, and the government is theoretically liable for compensation for damages, though recovery is difficult in practice. As of fiscal year 2025, there were 6,543 active secrecy orders. Critically, the Act applies to all patent applications -- not just those from government contractors or defense projects. Approximately twelve inventions per year from entirely private, non-government-affiliated inventors are placed under secrecy orders. The Defense Counterintelligence and Security Agency (formerly DTSA) coordinates patent security reviews across the Department of Defense. While conspiracy theorists often claim that revolutionary energy or medical technologies are being suppressed, the actual categories on the review list focus on areas with direct military application: nuclear weapons, cryptography, stealth technology, advanced sensors, and similar defense-relevant fields.

## 10. Voice of God Technology -- LRAD, Microwave Auditory Effect, Military Research

"Voice of God" technology refers to several real and theoretical systems capable of projecting sound to specific individuals or areas, drawing on two distinct scientific phenomena. The first is the Long Range Acoustic Device (LRAD), a commercially available directional sound cannon manufactured by Genasys Inc. that can project intelligible speech or pain-inducing tones over distances exceeding 1,000 meters; LRAD units have been deployed by U.S. military forces in Iraq for crowd control and perimeter defense, and by domestic law enforcement during protests. The second, more exotic basis is the microwave auditory effect (also called the Frey effect), discovered by American neuroscientist Allan H. Frey and first published in the Journal of Applied Physiology in 1962. Frey demonstrated that pulsed microwave radiation could produce audible clicks, buzzes, hisses, or knocking sensations perceived inside the human head without any receiving device, at distances up to approximately 100 meters from the transmitter. The accepted mechanism is thermoelastic expansion: each microwave pulse causes a minuscule temperature rise (on the order of 10^-5 degrees Celsius) in brain tissue, generating a pressure wave that propagates through the skull to the cochlea. The U.S. military has investigated weaponized applications of this effect; a 2003 U.S. Navy patent (US Patent 6,470,214) described a device for "communicating via the microwave auditory effect," and the U.S. Army's former website included a page on "Voice-to-Skull" (V2K) devices (later removed). However, no V2K weapon system has been openly fielded or confirmed as operational by any military. The Frey effect has been cited in connection with the "Havana Syndrome" incidents affecting U.S. diplomats, though investigations have not conclusively established microwave weapons as the cause. In conspiracy circles, "Voice of God" technology is conflated with mind control and Project Blue Beam narratives, with claims that governments routinely use it to implant thoughts -- assertions far beyond anything demonstrated in Frey's controlled laboratory experiments.

## 12. Stargate Technology -- Actual Stargate Project (Remote Viewing) vs. Portal Conspiracy

The name "Stargate" in this context refers to two very different things: a real, declassified U.S. government program and a fictional concept from conspiracy lore. The actual Stargate Project was a secret U.S. Army unit established in 1977 at Fort Meade, Maryland, jointly administered by the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA) and Stanford Research Institute (SRI), to investigate the potential military and intelligence applications of "remote viewing" -- the claimed psychic ability to perceive distant locations, objects, or events without physical travel. Over more than two decades, approximately $20 million was spent on the Stargate Project and related programs (including predecessor projects known as Sun Streak, Grill Flame, and Center Lane), with about 23 remote viewers and over 40 total personnel serving at various times. In 1995, following the Cold War's end, the CIA commissioned the American Institutes for Research (AIR) to conduct an independent evaluation; the resulting report acknowledged the program's existence but concluded that remote viewing had not produced any "actionable intelligence" for any intelligence operation and that "it remains unclear whether the existence of a paranormal phenomenon, remote viewing, has been demonstrated." The CIA terminated the program based on these findings. All of this is documented in declassified files available through the CIA's Reading Room. The conspiracy version of "Stargate technology" is entirely different: it refers to alleged physical portal devices -- inspired by the 1994 film "Stargate" and its TV franchise -- that conspiracy theorists claim the U.S. government operates for instantaneous travel between locations or dimensions. Some narratives link these portals to ancient artifacts, alien technology, or the aforementioned Looking Glass/Project Pegasus claims. There is no evidence whatsoever for the existence of portal technology; the actual Stargate Project was a psychic research program that was ultimately judged unsuccessful and shut down.

## 13. Teleportation -- Quantum Teleportation Reality vs. Full-Body Claims

Quantum teleportation is a real, experimentally verified protocol in quantum physics, first proposed theoretically in 1993 by Charles Bennett and colleagues, and since demonstrated with photons, atoms, and trapped ions across increasing distances. The process transfers the quantum state (the complete quantum information) of one particle to another particle at a distant location, using entanglement and classical communication -- but crucially, it transfers only information, not matter or energy. The original particle's state is destroyed in the process (consistent with the no-cloning theorem), and classical information must be sent at or below the speed of light, so quantum teleportation cannot achieve faster-than-light communication. As of the mid-2020s, quantum teleportation has been demonstrated over distances exceeding 1,400 kilometers using the Chinese Micius satellite, and the technology is foundational to quantum networking and quantum computing. However, the gap between quantum teleportation and the science-fiction concept of "beaming" a human being from one location to another is effectively infinite with current understanding. To teleport a person, one would need to scan, encode, transmit, and perfectly reconstruct the quantum states of all approximately 10^28 atoms in the human body -- a quantity of information so vast that it exceeds the capacity of any conceivable technology. Beyond the information problem, there are unresolved philosophical and physical questions about whether consciousness and identity would survive such a process. Conspiracy communities on the "Great Awakening Map" claim that full-body teleportation already exists as suppressed technology, often citing Project Pegasus or alleged reverse-engineered alien transport systems. These claims have no basis in any published physics, no experimental evidence, and no credible witnesses; they represent a fundamental misunderstanding of what quantum teleportation actually accomplishes.

## 14. Directed Energy Weapons (DEW) -- Military Development and Paradise CA Fire Conspiracy

Directed energy weapons (DEWs) are real military systems that use concentrated electromagnetic energy -- including high-energy lasers (HEL) and high-power microwaves (HPM) -- to damage or destroy targets. The U.S. military currently fields 21 laser weapon systems with average power ranging from a few watts to 60 kilowatts. In 2014, the Navy deployed the AN/SEQ-3 Laser Weapon System (LaWS) aboard the USS Ponce, and in 2023 the Army took delivery of the first platoon of Directed Energy Maneuver-Short Range Air Defense (DE M-SHORAD) systems -- 50 kW lasers mounted on Stryker armored vehicles designed to counter drones, rockets, artillery, and mortars. The Department of Defense's High Energy Laser Scaling Initiative (HELSI) has demonstrated 300 kW lasers in three distinct architectures, and the Navy plans to accelerate deployment of the HELIOS system on destroyers in the FY2026-2027 timeframe to counter drone swarm attacks. Despite this legitimate military development, conspiracy theories have alleged that DEWs were used to start or steer wildfires, particularly the 2018 Camp Fire that destroyed Paradise, California, the 2023 Maui wildfires, and the 2025 Los Angeles wildfires. These claims circulate on social media with images and videos purporting to show "beams from the sky." However, the California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection determined that the Camp Fire was caused by a faulty Pacific Gas & Electric transmission line, and fact-checking organizations have repeatedly shown that the circulating images are unrelated, miscaptioned, or fabricated. Weapons experts have pointed out that igniting vegetation from a distance would require enormous power levels delivered from a large platform, that high-energy laser beams are invisible to the naked eye (contradicting the "visible beam" videos), and that current DEW systems are designed for precision strikes against small, fast-moving military targets -- not for setting widespread fires across thousands of acres.

## 15. Smart Dust -- Actual DARPA Research vs. Conspiracy Claims

Smart dust is a legitimate concept in microelectromechanical systems (MEMS) research, originally proposed to DARPA by researchers at UC Berkeley in 1997 and funded in 1998. The concept envisions networks of tiny autonomous computing and sensing platforms -- ideally sub-millimeter in scale, no larger than a grain of sand -- capable of detecting environmental variables such as light, temperature, sound, vibration, magnetism, or the presence of chemical agents, and wirelessly transmitting that data to larger computer systems. The original Berkeley project, led by Professor Kristofer Pister, successfully produced working "motes" smaller than a grain of rice, and the technology has since inspired research into applications including weather monitoring (sensors dispersed in the atmosphere), forest fire detection, smart city infrastructure, structural health monitoring for turbine blades and bridges, and military battlefield surveillance. The U.S. Air Force published a research paper in 2007 examining smart dust's potential for "battlespace persistent surveillance." However, the technology remains far from its theoretical vision: current devices are more accurately described as "smart pebbles" rather than "smart dust," as the miniaturization challenges of packing sensors, processors, power sources, and wireless transceivers into truly dust-sized particles remain formidable. In conspiracy circles, smart dust has been conflated with claims about nano-scale surveillance particles secretly dispersed through chemtrails, vaccines, or food supplies to track and monitor the population. These claims dramatically overstate the technology's actual capabilities and current state of development. There is no evidence that smart dust has been weaponized for covert mass surveillance, and fact-checking organizations have debunked related claims such as the assertion that COVID-19 vaccines contain microchips or nanobots -- the lipid nanoparticles in mRNA vaccines are simple fat-based delivery vehicles for RNA, not computing devices. The actual gap between "grain of rice" sensor motes in a lab and the conspiracy vision of invisible, self-powered, AI-equipped surveillance particles dispersed by the billions is enormous.
