## 1. David Icke

David Vaughan Icke (born April 29, 1952, in Leicester, England) is a former professional footballer and BBC sports broadcaster who became one of the world's most prominent conspiracy theorists. He began his media career in 1981 as a sports presenter on BBC's Newsnight, co-hosted the flagship programme Grandstand in 1983, and appeared on the first episode of BBC Breakfast Time presenting sports news. His BBC contract was terminated in 1990 due to his outspoken opposition to the poll tax, and shortly after, while serving as national spokesperson for the Green Party, he visited a psychic who he says told him he had been placed on Earth for a purpose and would receive messages from the spirit world. His infamous 1991 appearance on the BBC's Wogan show, in which he claimed to be "a son of the godhead" wearing a turquoise tracksuit, made him a figure of national ridicule in Britain. Over the following decade he published a string of books developing an elaborate conspiratorial worldview, including The Robots' Rebellion (1994), And the Truth Shall Set You Free (1995), The Biggest Secret (1999), and Children of the Matrix (2001). His 533-page The Biggest Secret is considered the "Rosetta Stone" of his work, introducing the theory that a race of interdimensional reptilian beings -- the "Babylonian Brotherhood" -- has controlled human bloodlines and global power structures for millennia through shape-shifting hybrids occupying positions of elite authority. In Human Race Get Off Your Knees: The Lion Sleeps No More (2010), Icke expanded his cosmology with the "Moon Matrix" theory, proposing that the Moon is a spacecraft and interdimensional portal used by reptilians to manipulate Earth's collective consciousness. He has authored over 20 books in total, with later works including Infinite Love Is the Only Truth, Everything Else Is Illusion (2005) and The Trigger (2019). Critics have accused Icke of antisemitism and Holocaust denial, particularly for his endorsement of The Protocols of the Elders of Zion and his identification of the Rothschild family as reptilian bloodlines; his book And the Truth Shall Set You Free was criticized for arguing that Holocaust denial should be taught in schools. In November 2022, the Dutch government banned Icke from entering the Netherlands, and this ban was extended to the entire 26-country Schengen visa-free area for two years; he was also removed from major social media platforms including YouTube, Facebook, and Twitter. Despite these bans and deplatforming, his global following has remained large and loyal, and he continues to lecture and publish from his own platforms.

## 2. Alex Jones

Alexander Emric Jones (born February 11, 1974, in Dallas, Texas) is an American far-right radio host, conspiracy theorist, and founder of the media platform InfoWars. Jones began his career around 1993 hosting a call-in show on Austin, Texas public access television (PACT/ACTV) near the end of his senior year of high school, influenced by the broadcasts of William Cooper. In 1996 he moved to radio, hosting The Final Edition on KJFK (98.9 FM) in Austin, and in 1999 tied for "Best Austin Talk Radio Host" in The Austin Chronicle reader poll before being fired for refusing to broaden his topics. He subsequently launched InfoWars, which he built over more than 25 years into a multimillion-dollar enterprise primarily funded by sales of dietary supplements and survivalist products. One of his most famous early exploits was his July 2000 infiltration of Bohemian Grove, the secretive annual retreat of elite men in the California redwoods; Jones and cameraman Mike Hanson secretly recorded the "Cremation of Care" ceremony, which Jones characterized as a ritualistic event linked to the New World Order, releasing the documentary Dark Secrets: Inside Bohemian Grove that same year. Jones's career-defining legal catastrophe centered on his repeated claims that the December 2012 Sandy Hook Elementary School massacre, which killed 20 children and 6 adults, was a "giant hoax" and a "false flag" operation with "crisis actors" faking their grief. Families of victims sued Jones for defamation, and in 2022 juries rendered devastating verdicts: a Connecticut jury awarded $965 million in compensatory damages to eight Sandy Hook families, with a judge later adding $473 million in punitive damages, while a Texas jury separately awarded $49.3 million. In total, Jones was found liable for approximately $1.4 billion in damages. He filed for personal bankruptcy in late 2022, and the U.S. Supreme Court in October 2025 rejected his appeal of the defamation judgment. The Sandy Hook families offered a settlement for a minimum of $85 million, but as of mid-2025, Jones was accused of hiding money from the families, and the collection process remained ongoing. Jones also appeared in two Richard Linklater films (Waking Life and A Scanner Darkly) and has been a prominent promoter of conspiracy theories ranging from 9/11 "inside job" claims to the "New World Order" to COVID-19 misinformation.

## 3. Jordan Maxwell

Jordan Maxwell (born Russell Joseph Pine, December 28, 1940 -- died March 23, 2022) was an American self-taught researcher into occultism, religious philosophy, secret societies, and symbology who became widely known as "the Godfather of conspiracy research." He reportedly became drawn to these subjects in 1959 as a teenager, and over the following decades he developed an extensive body of work exploring what he described as the hidden influence of secret societies on religion, government, and culture. His research focused on connections between ancient mythology, astrology, and modern power structures, with particular emphasis on Masonic and occult symbolism embedded in corporate logos, national flags, legal language, and religious iconography. Maxwell authored numerous books, including Matrix of Power: Secrets of World Control, The Blood Covenant, and The Occult Connection, and he was a prolific lecturer who appeared in countless documentaries, radio shows, and online videos. He was a significant influence on many later conspiracy researchers: David Icke acknowledged that Maxwell's "contribution to the exposure of especially satanic symbolism used by the Cult has been immense," and his research laid groundwork for the symbolic analysis that became central to conspiracy culture on the internet. Despite never holding formal academic credentials in his fields of study, Maxwell gained a devoted following among those interested in alternative narratives of history and spirituality. He died on March 23, 2022, at the age of 82; the cause of death was not publicly disclosed, though no foul play was indicated. His legacy endures through his extensive video archive, his books, and his influence on writers and researchers within the alternative media and counterculture movements, where his ideas about hidden symbolic language in law, religion, and commerce continue to circulate widely.

## 4. Bill Cooper

Milton William "Bill" Cooper (May 6, 1943 -- November 5, 2001) was an American conspiracy theorist, radio broadcaster, and author best known for his 1991 book Behold a Pale Horse and his long-running shortwave radio program The Hour of the Time. Cooper claimed to have served in U.S. Naval Intelligence, stating he had been part of a briefing team for Admiral Bernard A. Clarey, though public records confirmed only standard Navy service with a rating of E-5 (Petty Officer Second Class) including a tour of duty in Vietnam. His book Behold a Pale Horse became one of the most influential texts in American conspiracy culture, weaving together claims about UFOs, secret government, the Illuminati, the Federal Reserve, AIDS as a bioweapon, and a New World Order agenda; the book has been described as routinely topping lists of the most shoplifted books in America and became enormously influential in hip-hop culture, with generations of rappers citing it as formative reading. From 1992 until his death in November 2001, Cooper broadcast The Hour of the Time from a studio in his home atop a hill in Eagar, Arizona, via shortwave radio and satellite. His most celebrated broadcast series was Mystery Babylon, an exhaustive multi-part exploration of secret societies, ancient mystery religions, the Knights Templar, Freemasonry, and their alleged ongoing influence on world events -- it remains widely circulated online to this day. On June 28, 2001, Cooper made a broadcast in which he commented on a televised interview of Osama bin Laden and predicted that bin Laden would soon be "blamed" for a major attack on a large U.S. city, adding "but don't you believe it" -- a prediction that, after the September 11 attacks weeks later, cemented his legendary status in conspiracy circles. Cooper was killed on November 5, 2001, during a confrontation with Apache County sheriff's deputies who were attempting to serve an arrest warrant on charges of aggravated assault with a deadly weapon and tax evasion; in a 17-officer operation, Cooper shot one deputy before being killed by return fire. He had emailed associates beforehand stating his intention to resist arrest. Cooper's influence was foundational: he was a direct influence on Alex Jones, who participated in Cooper's early radio shows, and his work presaged many conspiracy themes that would dominate internet culture for decades.

## 5. Bob Lazar

Robert Scott Lazar (born January 26, 1959, in Coral Gables, Florida) is an American who, since 1989, has claimed to have worked on reverse-engineering extraterrestrial technology at a secret facility he calls "S-4" near Area 51 in the Nevada desert. In May 1989, Lazar appeared in a landmark interview with investigative reporter George Knapp on Las Vegas TV station KLAS, initially under the pseudonym "Dennis," claiming that his job at S-4 involved studying the propulsion systems of nine flying saucers of extraterrestrial origin. He described the craft as using a fuel source based on a stable isotope of Element 115 (ununpentium, later named Moscovium when it was synthesized in 2003), which he said generated a gravity wave that allowed the craft to warp space-time for propulsion. While Element 115 was indeed later added to the periodic table, the synthesized isotopes proved highly unstable, decaying in fractions of a second -- contradicting Lazar's claim of a stable isotope. Lazar's credibility has been a subject of intense debate: he claims to hold master's degrees from MIT and the California Institute of Technology, but neither institution has records of his attendance; he stated he was hired by defense contractor EG&G, which denied having records of him; and while he claimed to have been a physicist at Los Alamos National Laboratory, investigation revealed he had been a technician employed by a subcontractor, not a staff physicist. In 1990, Lazar was arrested for involvement in the operation of a Nevada brothel. He was further convicted of pandering in the same case. Despite these credibility issues, Lazar's story has remained remarkably consistent over more than 35 years, and his claims received renewed mainstream attention through the 2018 documentary Bob Lazar: Area 51 & Flying Saucers directed by Jeremy Corbell and, most significantly, his 2019 appearance on The Joe Rogan Experience podcast (episode #1315), which garnered tens of millions of views. Supporters point to the fact that a Los Alamos phone directory from the 1980s did list his name, and that some aspects of his descriptions of the Area 51 facility layout have been corroborated by later disclosures. His story remains one of the most debated and culturally influential UFO claims in history.

## 6. Phil Schneider

Philip Schneider (April 23, 1947 -- January 1996) was an American self-proclaimed geological and structural engineer who gained significant attention in conspiracy circles during the mid-1990s for his extraordinary claims about secret underground military bases and alleged extraterrestrial encounters. Schneider claimed to have been involved in the construction of deep underground military bases (DUMBs) for the U.S. government, asserting that he had worked on 13 such facilities over the course of his career. His most dramatic claim concerned an alleged 1979 incident at the Dulce Base in New Mexico, where he said his drilling team accidentally breached an underground cavern inhabited by extraterrestrial beings, triggering a violent firefight between U.S. military personnel and the aliens that resulted in approximately 60 military deaths. He displayed what he said were injuries from the encounter -- a burned hand and missing fingers -- which he attributed to an alien weapon. Schneider further alleged that the U.S. government had been collaborating with alien species since the 1950s in technology exchange programs, and that trillions of dollars in "black budget" funds were being used to build underground facilities and develop exotic technologies. He went on a public lecture tour in 1995-1996, delivering these claims at conferences and public events. In January 1996, Schneider was found dead in his Wilsonville, Oregon apartment. The Clackamas County coroner's office ruled his death a suicide by strangulation (using a rubber catheter hose wrapped around his neck). However, his ex-wife Cynthia Drayer and many supporters contested this ruling, pointing to what they considered suspicious circumstances: his physical disabilities (from his alleged injuries) that they argued would have made self-strangulation implausible, the absence of key documents from his apartment, his claims of having survived multiple prior assassination attempts, and the timing shortly after his public disclosures. No physical evidence from the Dulce Base incident or any supposed underground facility has ever surfaced, no independent verification of his engineering credentials has been produced, and mainstream investigators consider his claims to be unsubstantiated. Nevertheless, Schneider has become a legendary figure in UFO and conspiracy communities, and his lecture videos continue to circulate widely online.

## 7. Dolores Cannon

Dolores Eilene Cannon (April 15, 1931 -- October 18, 2014) was an American author, self-trained hypnotherapist, and publisher who became one of the most influential figures in the New Age and metaphysical communities through her development of the Quantum Healing Hypnosis Technique (QHHT) and her prolific authorship of books based on information obtained during hypnotic regression sessions. Born in St. Louis, Missouri, Cannon began exploring hypnosis in the 1960s alongside her husband, a career Navy man who used hypnosis for weight loss and smoking cessation, but it was not until the 1970s that she began conducting past-life regressions and discovering what she interpreted as evidence of reincarnation, extraterrestrial contact, and multidimensional existence. She originally called her method "advanced past life regression" before renaming it Quantum Healing Hypnosis Technique (QHHT) around 2002, a method she claimed allowed her to communicate directly with a client's "Subconscious" (which she distinguished from the Freudian subconscious) to access past-life memories and facilitate physical healing. Over a career spanning more than 45 years, she published 19 books, including the five-volume Convoluted Universe series, which she described as a compendium of information too broad and diverse to categorize into specific areas -- covering topics ranging from the origins of humanity to parallel universes, the nature of reality, UFOs, and the mechanics of reincarnation. Her book The Three Waves of Volunteers and the New Earth (2011) presented one of her most popular concepts: that three distinct "waves" of souls have volunteered to incarnate on Earth to help humanity through a period of transformation and dimensional shift. The First Wave (people in their late 40s-60s at time of publication) came directly from "Source" and had the hardest time adjusting, often feeling alienated and wanting to "go home." The Second Wave (late 20s-30s) served as living "antennas" channeling higher-frequency energy onto Earth without necessarily needing to accomplish specific tasks. The Third Wave consisted of exceptional children and teenagers born with what Cannon described as enhanced DNA compatible with a "New Earth" frequency. She trained thousands of QHHT practitioners worldwide before her death in October 2014, and the technique continues to be taught through the official QHHT organization. Her other notable works include the Nostradamus trilogy, Keepers of the Garden, and The Custodians: Beyond Abduction.

## 8. Edgar Cayce

Edgar Cayce (March 18, 1877 -- January 3, 1945) was an American clairvoyant and psychic who became known as "The Sleeping Prophet" for his practice of entering a self-induced trance state and delivering detailed psychic readings while apparently asleep, producing 14,306 documented readings over the course of his life that were meticulously transcribed by his secretary, Gladys Davis. Born in Hopkinsville, Kentucky, Cayce displayed unusual abilities from childhood, including what he described as seeing visions and communicating with deceased relatives. His readings began as medical diagnoses -- with no formal medical training, he would enter a trance and prescribe detailed treatments for individuals who were often hundreds of miles away -- and his accuracy in these health readings brought him national attention as early as the 1910s. Over time, the scope of his readings expanded dramatically to encompass past lives, reincarnation, dream interpretation, spiritual development, ancient civilizations, and prophecy. His readings on Atlantis are among the most famous: Cayce described it as a highly advanced technological civilization that utilized crystal-based energy systems, flying vehicles, and sophisticated medical techniques, and which experienced three major destructions, the final one occurring around 10,000 BCE. He claimed that many of his contemporary clients had lived past lives in Atlantis and that records of the lost civilization were hidden in a "Hall of Records" beneath the Sphinx in Egypt. In 1931, Cayce founded the Association for Research and Enlightenment (ARE) in Virginia Beach, Virginia, which continues to operate as the institutional steward of his legacy, housing the complete archive of his readings, a research library, a health spa based on principles from the readings, and Atlantic University, which offers degree programs in transpersonal studies. The ARE has kept Cayce's work in print and in active study for decades. Two major sympathetic biographies -- There Is a River by Thomas Sugrue (1942) and Edgar Cayce: The Sleeping Prophet by Jess Stearn (1967) -- brought his story to mass audiences. While mainstream science and medicine reject his methods as having no empirical basis, Cayce's influence on the New Age movement has been immense: his ideas about reincarnation, holistic health, meditation, and Atlantis became foundational concepts in alternative spirituality.

## 9. Helena Blavatsky

Helena Petrovna Blavatsky (August 12, 1831 -- May 8, 1891) was a Russian-born occultist, author, and co-founder of the Theosophical Society who became one of the most influential figures in Western esotericism and a foundational architect of the modern New Age movement. Born into an aristocratic family in Yekaterinoslav (now Dnipro, Ukraine), Blavatsky led an extraordinarily varied early life that included stints as a circus horse rider, professional pianist, and businesswoman before embarking on extensive travels through the Americas, Europe, India, and Tibet, during which she claimed to have studied with hidden spiritual masters ("Mahatmas") who preserved ancient esoteric wisdom. In 1875, she co-founded the Theosophical Society in New York City with Henry Steel Olcott and William Quan Judge, describing Theosophy as "the synthesis of science, religion and philosophy" and claiming it revived the "Ancient Wisdom" underlying all the world's religions. Her first major work, Isis Unveiled (1877), challenged both established science and orthodox religion, but it was her magnum opus, The Secret Doctrine: The Synthesis of Science, Religion and Philosophy (1888), that became her most enduring and controversial legacy. The Secret Doctrine is a massive two-volume work: the first volume, "Cosmogenesis," presents an elaborate account of the origin and evolution of the universe; the second, "Anthropogenesis," describes the origin and evolution of humanity through a schema of "Root Races" stretching back millions of years. According to Blavatsky, the First Root Race was ethereal and existed on an "Imperishable Sacred Land"; the Second inhabited Hyperborea; the Third -- the first to be truly physical and human -- existed on the lost continent of Lemuria; the Fourth developed on Atlantis; and the Fifth (current) Root Race is the Aryan race, with a Sixth and Seventh yet to come. This racial schema has been widely criticized as pseudoscientific and has been accused of contributing to racist ideologies; historian Michael Marrus noted that Blavatsky's racial ideas "could be easily misused," and elements of her work influenced Ariosophy and were appropriated by some figures connected to National Socialism. Nevertheless, Blavatsky's Theosophical doctrines had an enormous positive impact in introducing Hindu and Buddhist ideas to Western audiences and spawned over 100 derivative esoteric movements, including Rudolf Steiner's Anthroposophy, the Church Universal and Triumphant, the I AM Activity, and the broader New Age movement. She died of influenza in London in 1891, but the Theosophical Society she founded remains active worldwide.

## 10. Aleister Crowley

Aleister Crowley (born Edward Alexander Crowley, October 12, 1875, in Royal Leamington Spa, England -- died December 1, 1947, in Hastings) was an English occultist, ceremonial magician, poet, novelist, mountaineer, and painter who founded the religion of Thelema and became one of the most notorious and influential figures in Western esotericism. Born into a wealthy and devoutly Plymouth Brethren family, Crowley rebelled against his strict religious upbringing and was drawn to the occult, being initiated in 1898 into the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn -- the most prestigious magical order of the Victorian era -- under the name Frater Perdurabo. He rapidly advanced through its grades but clashed with its leaders, and in 1907, together with George Cecil Jones, he founded the A.'.A.'. (Argenteum Astrum) as a successor organization incorporating Thelemic principles. In 1904, while in Cairo, Egypt, Crowley claimed to have received a communication from a praeterhuman intelligence called Aiwass, who dictated The Book of the Law (Liber AL vel Legis), containing the central axiom of Thelema: "Do what thou wilt shall be the whole of the Law," complemented by "Love is the law, love under will." Contrary to common misinterpretation, this was not an exhortation to hedonism but rather an injunction to discover and pursue one's "True Will" -- one's essential purpose in the cosmos. In 1912, Crowley was initiated into the Ordo Templi Orientis (O.T.O.), a German-based occult fraternal order whose rituals were largely based on Freemasonry; he rose to lead its British branch and substantially revised its rituals along Thelemic lines, eventually becoming the organization's international head. In 1920, he established the Abbey of Thelema, a religious commune in Cefalu, Sicily, where he lived with various followers; when a young Oxford undergraduate died there (reportedly from drinking contaminated local water), the British tabloid press launched a campaign of vilification, with John Bull magazine dubbing Crowley "the wickedest man in the world" -- a label that has clung to him ever since. He was expelled from Italy by Mussolini in 1923. Crowley's influence on conspiracy theories is significant though largely indirect: his association with elite secret societies, his deliberate cultivation of a diabolical public image, his connections to British intelligence (he is alleged to have done work for MI5 and MI6), and his association with prominent figures like rocket scientist Jack Parsons and, through Parsons, a pre-Scientology L. Ron Hubbard, have made him a recurring figure in conspiracy narratives about occult elites controlling world events. His philosophical and magical influence extends to Gerald Gardner (the founder of Wicca), the broader Neopagan movement, chaos magick, and, through cultural osmosis, rock music -- Led Zeppelin's Jimmy Page was an avid Crowley collector and Crowley's face appears on the cover of the Beatles' Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band.

## 11. Manly P. Hall

Manly Palmer Hall (March 18, 1901 -- August 29, 1990) was a Canadian-born American author, lecturer, astrologer, and mystic who became one of the most prolific and respected writers on ancient philosophy, symbolism, and esoteric traditions in the 20th century. Born to Louise Palmer Hall (a chiropractor and member of the Rosicrucian Fellowship) and William S. Hall (a dentist), he moved to Los Angeles as a young man and began lecturing on philosophical and mystical subjects while still in his teens. His magnum opus, The Secret Teachings of All Ages: An Encyclopedic Outline of Masonic, Hermetic, Qabbalistic and Rosicrucian Symbolical Philosophy, was published in 1928 when Hall was only 27 years old. This massive, lavishly illustrated folio volume -- running to nearly 250,000 words -- remains one of the most comprehensive single-volume surveys of ancient mystery traditions, symbolism, and esoteric philosophy ever produced, covering subjects from the Egyptian mysteries to Pythagorean mathematics, from Qabbalistic cosmology to the symbolism of the Tarot. Remarkably, although the book discusses Freemasonry in extensive detail, Hall was not himself a Mason when he wrote it; he did not petition for membership until 1954, when he was initiated into Jewel Lodge No. 374 in San Francisco, subsequently taking the Scottish Rite degrees and eventually receiving the 33rd degree -- the highest honor in Scottish Rite Freemasonry -- on December 8, 1973, some 45 years after publishing his masterwork. In 1934, Hall founded the Philosophical Research Society (PRS) in the Los Feliz neighborhood of Los Angeles, a nonprofit educational center dedicated to cross-cultural studies in religion, mythology, and philosophy, which he led for the rest of his life. Over his 70-year career, he delivered thousands of lectures and published over 150 volumes on subjects including astrology, dream interpretation, Buddhism, comparative religion, and American political philosophy. Hall died on August 29, 1990, at the age of 89; the cause was officially listed as heart failure, but the circumstances were suspicious -- investigators found that his personal assistant, Daniel Fritz, had arranged for Hall to rewrite his will only six days before his death, and the LAPD investigated but no charges were ever filed. Hall's work continues to be widely cited in both mainstream esoteric scholarship and conspiracy communities, where The Secret Teachings of All Ages is treated as an authoritative reference on the symbolism of secret societies. The Philosophical Research Society remains active, preserving his lectures, essays, and rare book collections.

## 12. Zecharia Sitchin

Zecharia Sitchin (July 11, 1920 -- October 9, 2010) was a Russian-born American author who proposed one of the most influential -- and most criticized -- ancient astronaut theories of the 20th century. Born in Baku, Azerbaijan SSR, and raised in Mandatory Palestine, Sitchin studied economics at the London School of Economics before becoming a journalist and editor in Israel, eventually settling in New York City. He taught himself to read Sumerian cuneiform and, in 1976, published The 12th Planet, the first of seven books in his "Earth Chronicles" series (followed by six companion volumes). His central thesis held that the Anunnaki -- deities appearing in ancient Sumerian, Akkadian, Assyrian, and Babylonian mythology -- were not gods but a race of extraterrestrials from a planet called Nibiru, which he claimed orbits the Sun on a 3,600-year elliptical path beyond Neptune. According to Sitchin, the Anunnaki arrived on Earth approximately 450,000 years ago seeking gold to repair their planet's atmosphere, and genetically engineered Homo sapiens by mixing their DNA with that of Homo erectus to create a slave labor force for mining operations -- hence making humanity a "slave species of the gods." He also identified Nibiru as a "12th planet" in the solar system (counting the Sun, Moon, and Pluto). Scholarly reception has been overwhelmingly negative: Sumerologists, Orientalists, and Assyriologists have dismissed his translations as fundamentally flawed, with scholar Michael Heiser producing extensive line-by-line critiques demonstrating that Sumerian texts "just don't support Sitchin's reconstruction." Astronomers have pointed out that a planet on the orbit Sitchin described would be gravitationally impossible and would have been detected. Despite this near-universal academic rejection, Sitchin's books sold millions of copies worldwide and were translated into more than 25 languages, and his Anunnaki/Nibiru framework became a foundational element of modern conspiracy culture, directly influencing later writers including Michael Tellinger and many internet-based theorists. Sitchin died in New York on October 9, 2010, and his ideas continue to circulate extensively in alternative media and on the Great Awakening Map.

## 13. Graham Hancock

Graham Bruce Hancock (born August 2, 1950, in Edinburgh, Scotland) is a British writer and journalist who became one of the most prominent and commercially successful proponents of the theory that an advanced civilization existed during the last Ice Age and was destroyed by a cataclysm around 12,000 years ago. Educated in sociology at Durham University, Hancock began his career as a mainstream journalist, working for The Times, The Sunday Times, The Independent, and The Guardian, co-editing New Internationalist magazine from 1976 to 1979, and serving as the East Africa correspondent for The Economist from 1981 to 1983. His early books were works of conventional development journalism, including Lords of Poverty (1989), a well-regarded critique of corruption in the international aid system. His career pivoted with The Sign and the Seal (1992), a quest for the lost Ark of the Covenant, and reached full alternative-history territory with Fingerprints of the Gods: The Evidence of Earth's Lost Civilization (1995), which argued that an advanced global civilization, possibly centered on what is now Antarctica, was destroyed by massive floods at the end of the last Ice Age around 10,500 BCE, and that survivors transmitted their astronomical and architectural knowledge to hunter-gatherer societies around the world, explaining the emergence of civilizations in Egypt, Mesopotamia, and the Americas. Hancock's theories incorporate the Younger Dryas impact hypothesis -- the controversial proposal by the Comet Research Group that a massive meteor bombardment approximately 12,900 years ago triggered catastrophic climate change that ended the Pleistocene. In November 2022, Hancock became the subject of intense international debate with the release of Ancient Apocalypse, a Netflix docuseries that became one of the platform's most-watched documentaries, in which he revisited archaeological sites around the world to argue his lost-civilization thesis. The Society for American Archaeology (SAA) issued an open letter condemning the show for its "disparaging remarks against archaeologists" and its alignment with what they characterized as racist pseudoarchaeological frameworks that strip Indigenous peoples of credit for their own monumental achievements. Archaeologist Flint Dibble, who debated Hancock on Joe Rogan's podcast in 2024, argued the show was "lacking in evidence" while "a plethora of evidence" contradicted Hancock's claims. Plans to film parts of the show's second season in the USA were cancelled following opposition from Indigenous groups. A second season covering further sites was released in late 2024. Hancock maintains that he is not an archaeologist but a journalist investigating possibilities that mainstream academia refuses to consider.

## 14. Erich von Daniken

Erich Anton Paul von Daniken (April 14, 1935 -- January 10, 2026) was a Swiss author who almost single-handedly popularized the ancient astronaut hypothesis and became one of the best-selling non-fiction authors in history. Born in Schaffhausen, Switzerland, von Daniken developed an interest in ancient mysteries and extraterrestrial possibilities while attending a Jesuit boarding school, and he wrote his groundbreaking book while working as a hotel manager in Davos. Early in his career, he was convicted on several counts of fraud and embezzlement and served time in prison, during which he reportedly wrote one of his books. His 1968 work, Chariots of the Gods? Unsolved Mysteries of the Past, proposed that the technologies and religions of many ancient civilizations were given to them by extraterrestrial visitors who were welcomed as gods. The book pointed to artifacts such as the Nazca Lines in Peru, the Egyptian pyramids, the Moai of Easter Island, and ancient religious texts as evidence of alien contact, arguing that ancient peoples lacked the capability to produce such feats on their own. Chariots of the Gods? became a global phenomenon, landing on The New York Times bestseller list and selling millions of copies, launching von Daniken into a career as a prolific author and globe-trotting lecturer. Over his lifetime he wrote 49 books, which sold 75 million copies and were translated into more than 30 languages. Scientists and academics categorized his work as pseudohistory, pseudoarchaeology, and pseudoscience, criticizing his selective use of evidence, misrepresentation of archaeological findings, and logical fallacies. Nevertheless, his cultural impact was enormous: his ideas directly inspired the History Channel's long-running series Ancient Aliens (debuting 2009), where he appeared as a frequent commentator; influenced Marvel Comics' The Eternals; provided conceptual foundations for films including Ridley Scott's Prometheus and the Indiana Jones franchise. He also co-founded the Archaeology, Astronautics and SETI Research Association (A.A.S. R.A.) and designed Mystery Park, a theme park in Interlaken, Switzerland, dedicated to ancient mysteries, which opened in 2003 but faced financial difficulties and was later reorganized as JungfrauPark. Von Daniken died on January 10, 2026, at Interlaken Hospital at the age of 90, from complications related to old age, leaving behind a legacy as the single most influential popularizer of the ancient astronaut theory.

## 15. Michael Tellinger

Michael Tellinger (born 1960 in Johannesburg, South Africa) is a South African author, politician, musician, explorer, and founder of the Ubuntu Party and the global Ubuntu Liberation Movement. Tellinger's early career was in the arts -- as part of the music duo "Stirling & Tellinger" he had several hit songs in South Africa -- before he turned his attention to alternative history, ancient civilizations, and fringe science. His 2006 book, Slave Species of the Gods: The Secret History of the Anunnaki and Their Mission on Earth, drew heavily on Zecharia Sitchin's ancient astronaut framework, arguing that the Anunnaki genetically engineered humans as a slave species for gold mining in southern Africa. In 2007, Tellinger claims to have discovered the ruins of ancient vanished civilizations in southern Africa, identifying millions of circular stone structures that he argues date back over 300,000 years and represent the largest concentration of stone ruins on Earth. His most publicized claim concerns the "Blaauboschkraal stone ruins" in Mpumalanga, South Africa, which mainstream archaeologists have identified as 16th-century cattle kraals (boundary markers), but which Tellinger promotes as "Adam's Calendar" -- an alien-built astronomical site at the center of a vast network of stone circles that he claims channeled energy in ancient times. He has authored several related books, including Temples of the African Gods (2009, with Johan Heine) and African Temples of the Anunnaki (2013). Beyond his alternative history work, Tellinger founded the Ubuntu Contributionism movement, a socio-political philosophy advocating for a world without money, based on the principle of community contribution rather than monetary exchange. He formalized this into the Ubuntu Party in South Africa and ran for political office. His book UBUNTU Contributionism: A Blueprint for Human Prosperity outlines a system in which all resources are shared equally and community members contribute their skills and talents without expectation of monetary compensation. The Ubuntu Liberation Movement claims members in over 200 countries. Academic archaeologists and historians consider Tellinger's ancient history claims to be pseudoarchaeology, noting that his interpretations of the South African stone ruins lack supporting evidence and contradict established archaeological dating. He remains a popular figure on the alternative history and conspiracy lecture circuit, frequently appearing at conferences alongside other figures referenced on the Great Awakening Map.
