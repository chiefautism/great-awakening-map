## 1. DMT (N,N-Dimethyltryptamine)

![DMT molecule structure](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/88/DMT.svg)

From 1990 to 1995, Dr. Rick Strassman conducted the first federally approved psychedelic research in over two decades at the University of New Mexico, administering approximately 400 doses of intravenous DMT to nearly 60 human volunteers in a rigorous clinical setting that included dose-response studies, tolerance studies, and receptor mechanism-of-action investigations ([Wikipedia - Rick Strassman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rick_Strassman), [Rick Strassman MD](https://www.rickstrassman.com/publications/the-spirit-molecule/)). Strassman's research revealed that DMT produced no tolerance development -- a remarkable pharmacological finding -- and that more than half of the volunteers reported profound encounters with seemingly autonomous nonhuman entities while in a fully dissociated state, leading him to coin the term "spirit molecule" because the substance's effects included visions, disembodied consciousness, powerful emotions, and feelings of overwhelming significance ([American Journal of Psychiatry Review](https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.ajp.159.8.1448)). His 2001 book *DMT: The Spirit Molecule* documented specific trip reports in extraordinary detail, with volunteers describing contact with beings variously perceived as machine elves (a term popularized by Terence McKenna), jesters, mantis-like insectoids, reptilian entities, and luminous feminine presences that communicated through telepathy and visual language ([IFLScience](https://www.iflscience.com/why-do-people-see-elves-and-other-entities-when-they-smoke-dmt-62234)).

A landmark 2020 survey conducted by Johns Hopkins researchers (Davis, Clifton, Weaver, Hurwitz, Johnson, and Griffiths) collected data from 2,561 individuals who reported entity encounters after inhaling N,N-DMT, finding that entity types included feminine phenotypes (24.2%), deities (17.0%), aliens (16.3%), creature-based entities including reptilian and insectoid beings (9.2%), mythological beings including machine elves (8.4%), and jesters (6.5%) ([PubMed - Davis et al. 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32345112/), [Journal of Psychopharmacology](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0269881120916143)). The majority of respondents described the experience as among the most meaningful of their lives, with 65% reporting joy, 63% trust, and 59% love; a large proportion came to believe the entities were autonomous beings existing independently of the experiencer's mind, and many reported lasting shifts away from atheism following the encounter ([New Atlas](https://newatlas.com/science/dmt-survey-psychedelic-atheism-johns-hopkins-alan-davis/)). A pivotal 2019 study published in *Scientific Reports* by Jimo Borjigin's team at the University of Michigan definitively confirmed that DMT is naturally synthesized in the mammalian brain, identifying INMT (indolethylamine-N-methyltransferase) transcripts in the cerebral cortex, pineal gland, and choroid plexus of both rats and humans, and demonstrating that extracellular DMT concentrations in the rat cerebral cortex were comparable to those of serotonin and other canonical monoamine neurotransmitters ([Nature - Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-45812-w), [ScienceDaily](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2019/06/190627113951.htm)). Critically, the study found that DMT levels surged in the rat visual cortex following experimentally induced cardiac arrest -- and this surge occurred independently of an intact pineal gland, challenging Rick Strassman's original hypothesis that the pineal was the primary source ([Michigan Medicine](https://www.michiganmedicine.org/health-lab/mystical-psychedelic-compound-found-normal-brains), [PsyPost](https://www.psypost.org/study-provides-evidence-that-dmt-is-produced-naturally-from-neurons-in-the-mammalian-brain/)).

The distinction between 5-MeO-DMT and N,N-DMT is pharmacologically and experientially profound: N,N-DMT is known for producing exceptionally vivid, complex visual imagery and entity encounters, while 5-MeO-DMT (found naturally in the venom of the Sonoran Desert toad *Bufo alvarius*, now reclassified *Incilius alvarius*) induces complete ego dissolution and a sense of merging with universal consciousness, working primarily through 5-HT1A receptor agonism rather than 5-HT2A dominance ([Wikipedia - 5-MeO-DMT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-MeO-DMT), [Beckley Retreats](https://www.beckleyretreats.com/blog/bufo-vs-ayahuasca)). Natural toad venom contains not just 5-MeO-DMT but also bufotenine, bufagins (cardioactive agents), catecholamines, and other compounds that create a distinct -- and potentially more dangerous -- pharmacological profile compared to synthetic 5-MeO-DMT ([DoubleBlind Mag](https://doubleblindmag.com/toad-venom-vs-synthetic-5-meo-dmt/)). Pharmahuasca refers to the practice of combining synthetic DMT with a pharmaceutical MAO inhibitor (typically harmaline or harmine) to render DMT orally active, mimicking the mechanism of traditional ayahuasca but with precisely controlled dosing; this approach carries serious risks including serotonin syndrome when combined with SSRIs or other serotonergic medications ([PMC - Pharmacological Interaction](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7678905/)).

## 2. Terence McKenna (1946-2000)

Terence Kemp McKenna was born on November 16, 1946, in Paonia, Colorado, and grew up to become one of the most influential psychedelic thinkers of the 20th century -- an ethnobotanist, mystic, author, and lecturer whose ideas about consciousness, evolution, and the nature of reality continue to reverberate through counterculture decades after his death ([Wikipedia - Terence McKenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terence_McKenna), [PsychonautWiki](https://psychonautwiki.org/wiki/Terence_McKenna)). In 1971, McKenna and his younger brother Dennis traveled to La Chorrera in the Colombian Amazon, where they conducted a radical psychedelic experiment involving psilocybin mushrooms and an attempt to use sound to bond tryptamine molecules to their own neural DNA -- an event McKenna later described in his 1993 memoir *True Hallucinations* as contact with an "alien intelligence" that communicated through Dennis and ultimately inspired the mathematical framework of Timewave Zero ([Dangerous Minds](https://dangerousminds.net/comments/timewave_zero_did_terence_mckenna_really_believe_in_all_that_2012_prophecy/), [Wikipedia - Terence McKenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terence_McKenna)). His 1975 book *The Invisible Landscape* (co-authored with Dennis) laid out the Timewave Zero theory in full, a mathematical model derived from the 64 hexagrams of the King Wen sequence of the I Ching that purported to map fractal patterns of "novelty" throughout history, predicting an acceleration toward a singularity of infinite complexity originally set for mid-November 2012, which McKenna later aligned with the end-date of the Mayan long-count calendar on December 21, 2012 ([Boing Boing](https://boingboing.net/2012/06/07/timewave-zero-did-terence-mck.html)).

The Stoned Ape hypothesis, first articulated in McKenna's 1992 book *Food of the Gods*, proposed that the evolution from *Homo erectus* to *Homo sapiens* was catalyzed by the consumption of *Psilocybe cubensis* mushrooms found in the dung of African cattle around 100,000 BCE; at low doses psilocybin would have enhanced visual acuity and hunting success, at medium doses it would have increased sexual arousal and social bonding, and at high doses it would have induced the development of language, imagination, and religious experience ([Wikipedia - Stoned Ape Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoned_ape_theory), [Psychology Today](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/the-leading-edge/202406/the-stoned-ape-theory-revisited)). While the hypothesis gained no traction in mainstream paleoanthropology due to its lack of fossil or archaeological evidence, it became foundational mythology within psychedelic culture and has been partially revisited by researchers examining the role of psychoactive substances in early human cognition ([HowStuffWorks](https://science.howstuffworks.com/life/evolution/stoned-ape-hypothesis.htm)). McKenna coined the concept of the "heroic dose" -- five dried grams of psilocybin mushrooms taken alone, on an empty stomach, in silent darkness with eyes closed -- as a protocol for achieving full visionary breakthrough, a formulation that became one of psychedelic culture's most enduring and influential prescriptions ([The Ethos](https://the-ethos.co/the-heroic-dose-terence-mckenna-psychedelic-culture/), [Microdosify](https://microdosify.com/blog/heroic-dose-protocol/)).

Dubbed the "Timothy Leary of the '90s" and the "intellectual voice of rave culture," McKenna became a central figure in the psychedelic rave and trance music scenes of the early 1990s, delivering spoken word performances at events and contributing vocal samples to albums by The Shamen, Shpongle, Spacetime Continuum, and Entheogenic, among others ([Terrence McKenna Archives](https://terencemckennaarchives.com/tag/rave-culture/), [Reality Sandwich](https://realitysandwich.com/terence-mckenna/)). He was remarkably prescient about technology, envisioning virtual reality as a "cyberdelic" medium that would allow humans to externalize and share their interior visions, essentially creating a form of visual telepathy; he also warned that artificial intelligence, if not developed with ethical foundations comparable to those of a Buddha, could pose existential threats to humanity ([Sociable](https://sociable.co/technology/terence-mckenna-ai/), [Doolly](https://www.doolly.com/blog/terence-mckenna-escalating-weirdness-singularity)). In May 1999, McKenna suffered a seizure and was diagnosed with glioblastoma multiforme, a highly aggressive brain tumor; although he reportedly worried the tumor might have been caused by his psychedelic use or 35 years of daily cannabis consumption, his doctors assured him there was no causal connection, and he died on April 3, 2000, at his home in San Rafael, California, at the age of 53 ([Wikipedia - Terence McKenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terence_McKenna), [Pevgrow](https://pevgrow.com/blog/en/terence-mckenna-psychedelic-visionary-and-legacy-of-magic-mushrooms/)). The 2012 prediction, of course, passed without incident, though Dennis McKenna -- today a respected ethnopharmacologist -- later acknowledged he was never much of a believer in his brother's apocalyptic timeline.

## 3. Psilocybin Clinical Research

The modern era of psilocybin clinical research was inaugurated by Roland Griffiths and colleagues at Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine with their 2006 landmark study published in *Psychopharmacology*, a rigorous double-blind investigation in which 30 hallucinogen-naive adults with regular spiritual practices received either psilocybin (30 mg/70 kg) or an active placebo (methylphenidate 40 mg/70 kg) in counterbalanced sessions spaced two months apart under comfortable, supportive conditions ([PubMed - Griffiths et al. 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16826400/), [MAPS Interview](https://maps.org/news/bulletin/johns-hopkins-psilocybin-studies-and-the-religious-experience-an-interview-with-roland-griffiths-ph-d/)). The results were striking: psilocybin occasioned complete mystical experiences (as measured by validated instruments) in the majority of participants, and at 14-month follow-up, 67% rated the experience among the top five most personally meaningful and spiritually significant events of their entire lives, with sustained positive changes in attitudes, mood, and behavior corroborated by community observers ([PMC - 14 Month Follow-Up](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3050654/)). This study single-handedly reopened the door for rigorous psychedelic research after decades of prohibition and directly inspired a wave of clinical trials at institutions worldwide.

Subsequent research at Johns Hopkins and New York University demonstrated that psilocybin-assisted therapy produced rapid, substantial, and enduring reductions in anxiety and depression in patients with life-threatening cancer diagnoses, with approximately 80% showing clinically significant decreases in distress at six-month follow-up ([Johns Hopkins Medicine](https://www.hopkinsmedicine.org/psychiatry/research/psychedelics-research)). The FDA granted Breakthrough Therapy designation to psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression in 2018 (for COMPASS Pathways' COMP360 formulation) and for major depressive disorder in 2019 (for the Usona Institute), signaling regulatory recognition of psilocybin's extraordinary therapeutic potential ([UTHealth - FDA Breakthrough](https://med.uth.edu/psychiatry/2024/06/03/fda-grants-breakthrough-therapy-designation-to-cyb003-a-deuterated-psilocybin-analog-being-investigated-as-an-adjunctive-treatment-for-major-depressive-disorder-mdd/)). COMPASS Pathways conducted the largest psilocybin clinical trial to date -- a phase 2b randomized controlled study with 233 patients across 22 sites in 10 countries -- finding that a single 25 mg dose of COMP360 psilocybin produced highly statistically significant reductions in depressive symptoms at three weeks (p<0.001) with durable response lasting up to 12 weeks, results published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* ([COMPASS Pathways](https://compasspathways.com/our-work/comp360-psilocybin-treatment-in-trd/)).

Imperial College London, under the leadership of Robin Carhart-Harris and David Nutt, has been a global hub for psilocybin neuroimaging research, producing foundational work on how psilocybin disrupts the default mode network and increases global brain connectivity; the institution now hosts a dedicated psychedelic research center ([AIMS Institute](https://www.aimsinstitute.net/psychedelics-for-medical-purposes-time-to-change-your-mind/)). Oregon voters passed Measure 109 in November 2020, making Oregon the first U.S. state to legalize psilocybin for supervised therapeutic use in licensed service centers, a regulatory framework that began accepting clients in 2023 ([AOL News](https://www.aol.com/news/oregon-first-state-legalize-therapeutic-psilocybin-magic-mushrooms-help-depression-225558233.html), [OPB](https://www.opb.org/article/2022/07/28/author-interview-michael-pollan-portland-oregon-psychedelic-drugs-psilocybin/)). Michael Pollan's 2018 bestseller *How to Change Your Mind: What the New Science of Psychedelics Teaches Us About Consciousness, Dying, Addiction, Depression, and Transcendence* brought this research to mainstream audiences and was adapted into a Netflix documentary series, significantly shifting public perception ([Michael Pollan](https://michaelpollan.com/books/how-to-change-your-mind/), [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/36613747-how-to-change-your-mind)). As of 2025, active clinical trials continue across multiple indications including treatment-resistant depression, major depressive disorder, cancer-related distress, anorexia, PTSD, substance use disorders, and various types of chronic pain, though the field faced setbacks when the FDA rejected MAPS/Lykos Therapeutics' MDMA-assisted therapy application for PTSD in 2024, raising questions about the regulatory pathway for all psychedelic-assisted therapies ([ClinicalTrials.gov](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06303739), [ScienceDirect - Meta-Analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S016517812400622X)).

## 4. Ayahuasca

Ayahuasca is a psychoactive decoction traditionally prepared from two Amazonian plants: the *Banisteriopsis caapi* vine, which contains the beta-carboline alkaloids harmine, harmaline, and tetrahydroharmine (THH), and the leaves of *Psychotria viridis* (chacruna), which contain N,N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT); the pharmacological genius of this combination lies in the fact that the beta-carbolines act as reversible inhibitors of monoamine oxidase-A (MAO-A), preventing the gut enzyme from breaking down DMT before it can cross the blood-brain barrier, thus rendering DMT orally active for a duration of 4-6 hours ([ICEERS - Ayahuasca Basic Info](https://www.iceers.org/ayahuasca-basic-info/), [Wikipedia - Ayahuasca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayahuasca), [PubMed - MAO Inhibitors](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6587171/)). The archaeological evidence for psychedelic plant use in South America extends back at least a millennium: a ritual bundle discovered in a cave in southwestern Bolivia, dated to approximately 1,000 CE and associated with the Tiwanaku civilization, contained carved snuffing trays, bone spatulas, and chemical traces of harmine, DMT, cocaine, and bufotenine -- representing the earliest direct chemical evidence of combined psychoactive plant use in the Americas, though this likely represents snuff rather than ayahuasca brew specifically ([PNAS - Archaeological Evidence](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1902174116), [Berkeley News](https://news.berkeley.edu/2019/05/06/ayahuasca-sacred-bundle/)). The first written account of ayahuasca appears in a 1737 report by Jesuit missionary Pablo Maroni describing its use for divination along the Napo River in Ecuador, and Manuel Villavicencio's 1858 *Geografia de la Republica del Ecuador* provided detailed descriptions of ayahuasca rituals among the Jivaro people ([Chacruna - Origins](https://chacruna.net/is-ayahuasca-possibly-less-than-five-hundred-years-old/)).

The Santo Daime church was founded in the 1930s in the Brazilian Amazonian state of Acre by Raimundo Irineu Serra -- known as Mestre Irineu -- a Black rubber tapper of Maranhense origin who, according to church tradition, received divine instruction from a female spirit he identified as the Virgin Mary during an ayahuasca vision in the forest, leading him to create a syncretic religion blending folk Catholicism, Kardecist Spiritism, African animism, and indigenous Amazonian shamanism ([Wikipedia - Santo Daime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santo_Daime), [Chacruna - Mestre Irineu](https://chacruna.net/mestre-irineu-ayahuasca/), [Queen of the Forest](https://queenoftheforest.org/traditions/santo-daime/mestre-irineu-raimundo-irineu-serra-founder-of-the-santo-daime-tradition/)). In the 1960s, Jose Gabriel da Costa (Mestre Gabriel), also a rubber tapper, founded the Uniao do Vegetal (UDV), a more centralized and doctrinally unified ayahuasca church that won a landmark U.S. Supreme Court case in 2006 (*Gonzales v. O Centro Espirita Beneficente Uniao do Vegetal*) affirming its right to import and use ayahuasca for religious purposes under the Religious Freedom Restoration Act. Iquitos, Peru, has become the global epicenter of ayahuasca tourism, with foreign entrepreneurs establishing dozens of retreat centers catering to Western seekers; however, this has created serious problems including overharvesting of *B. caapi* (which requires 3-10 years to mature), exploitation of indigenous knowledge, and safety concerns ([ICEERS - Ayahuasca Tourism](https://www.iceers.org/ayahuasca-tourism-who-how-where/), [Chacruna - Iquitos](https://chacruna.net/the-corona-crisis-a-view-from-the-ayahuasca-capital-of-iquitos/)).

Deaths associated with ayahuasca have drawn significant media attention: ICEERS documented 58 deaths attributed to ayahuasca consumption in media reports from 1994 to 2022, with 40% linked to tourist retreat centers in Amazonian countries ([ICEERS - Examining Deaths](https://www.iceers.org/examining-deaths-ayahuasca/)). Matthew Taylor Mellon II, the billionaire banking heir and cryptocurrency investor, died of a heart attack on April 16, 2018, in a Cancun hotel room reportedly after taking ayahuasca at a treatment facility where he had gone to address his OxyContin addiction while 88 days sober ([The Daily Beast](https://www.thedailybeast.com/report-matthew-mellon-tried-controversial-hallucinogenic-to-treat-addiction-before-he-died/), [Wikipedia - Matthew Mellon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_Mellon)). Dr. Gabor Mate, the renowned Hungarian-Canadian physician specializing in addiction and trauma, became one of the most prominent advocates for ayahuasca's therapeutic potential after initially being skeptical; he now describes it as an "antidote to Western psychological distress and cultural alienation" and has facilitated ayahuasca ceremonies as part of his therapeutic work, drawing on the premise that ayahuasca enables access to repressed traumatic material that underlies addictive behavior ([Dr. Gabor Mate](https://drgabormate.com/profound-power-amazonian-plant-respect-demands/), [Tim Ferriss Blog](https://tim.blog/2018/02/20/gabor-mate/), [Soltara](https://soltara.co/dr-gabor-mate/)). ICEERS, the International Center for Ethnobotanical Education Research and Service, founded in Barcelona in 2009, has provided legal support in 448 cases across 49 countries and publishes comprehensive technical reports on ayahuasca's pharmacology, legal status, and public health implications, noting that the ayahuasca decoction itself is not scheduled under international drug control conventions (as confirmed by the INCB) though national laws vary enormously ([ICEERS](https://www.iceers.org/), [ICEERS - Legal Status](https://www.iceers.org/ayahuasca-legal-status/)).

## 5. LSD (Lysergic Acid Diethylamide)

Albert Hofmann, a Swiss chemist working at Sandoz Laboratories in Basel, first synthesized lysergic acid diethylamide (LSD-25) on November 16, 1938, as the twenty-fifth compound in a series of ergot alkaloid derivatives being investigated as potential respiratory and circulatory stimulants, but the substance was set aside for five years after initial animal testing showed no particularly promising pharmacological effects ([Wikipedia - Albert Hofmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Hofmann), [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Hofmann)). On April 16, 1943, Hofmann accidentally absorbed a small amount of LSD through his fingertips while re-synthesizing the compound and experienced unusual perceptual disturbances; three days later, on April 19, 1943 -- now celebrated worldwide as Bicycle Day -- he deliberately ingested 250 micrograms (what he believed to be a threshold dose but was actually quite strong) and attempted to ride his bicycle home as the effects intensified, experiencing the world's first intentional LSD trip with vivid visual distortions, anxiety, and eventually profound perceptual transformation ([Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/culture/culture-news/bicycle-day-albert-hofmann-lsd-psychedelic-986279/), [Pearl Psychedelic Institute](https://www.pearlpsychedelicinstitute.org/post/the-meaning-and-significance-of-bicycle-day)). Sandoz began manufacturing LSD under the trade name Delysid in 1947, distributing it to qualified researchers and psychiatrists worldwide with a suggestion that therapists take the substance themselves to gain "insight into the world of ideas and sensations of mental patients" ([Wikipedia - History of LSD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_lysergic_acid_diethylamide)).

The CIA's MKUltra program, initiated in 1953 under the direction of chemist Sidney Gottlieb, made LSD the centerpiece of its mind control research, administering the substance to hundreds of unwitting subjects including CIA agents, military personnel, prisoners, mental patients, and members of the general public, often without their knowledge or consent ([HISTORY Channel](https://www.history.com/mkultra-operation-midnight-climax-cia-lsd-experiments)). Operation Midnight Climax, launched in 1954 and overseen by Federal Bureau of Narcotics agent George Hunter White (operating under the alias "Morgan Hall"), established CIA-run safe houses in San Francisco (at 225 Chestnut Street), Mill Valley, and New York City, where prostitutes recruited by White would lure unsuspecting men, spike their drinks with LSD, and the subjects would be observed and filmed through one-way mirrors while White and CIA operatives documented the effects -- a program that ran until 1963 when CIA Inspector General John Earman recommended its closure ([Wikipedia - Operation Midnight Climax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Midnight_Climax), [NPR](https://www.npr.org/transcripts/758989641), [Coffee or Die](https://www.coffeeordie.com/article/cia-midnight-climax)). The program was exposed by the Church Committee Senate investigation in 1975, and George Hunter White later wrote in a letter: "I toiled wholeheartedly in the vineyards because it was fun, fun, fun."

Timothy Leary, a Harvard psychology professor, first tried psilocybin mushrooms in Cuernavaca, Mexico in 1960 and was so transformed that he and colleague Richard Alpert (later Ram Dass) established the Harvard Psilocybin Project, transitioning to LSD research before both were fired from Harvard in 1963 amid controversy over their methodology and alleged distribution of psychedelics to undergraduates ([Wikipedia - Timothy Leary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timothy_Leary), [Harvard Psychology](https://psychology.fas.harvard.edu/people/timothy-leary)). Ken Kesey, who had first encountered LSD as a paid volunteer in CIA-funded experiments at the Menlo Park Veterans' Hospital, took the opposite approach from Leary's controlled settings: beginning in December 1965 in San Jose, California, Kesey and his Merry Pranksters hosted the Acid Tests -- public psychedelic happenings where attendees were given LSD and immersed in live music (by the Grateful Dead), light shows, and multimedia art -- democratizing the psychedelic experience and laying the foundations for the counterculture explosion ([Origins - Ohio State](https://origins.osu.edu/milestones/december-2015-acid-tests)). Augustus Owsley Stanley III ("Bear") was a self-taught chemist who set up his own laboratory and first succeeded in synthesizing crystalline LSD in February 1965, reportedly producing between 1 and 5 million doses of exceptionally pure LSD that fueled the San Francisco scene and funded the Grateful Dead's sound system ([HowStuffWorks](https://science.howstuffworks.com/lsd7.htm)). The U.S. criminalized LSD possession in October 1966, and its classification as a Schedule I substance in the 1970 Controlled Substances Act effectively ended legitimate research for decades.

The modern psychedelic research revival owes much to Rick Doblin, who founded the Multidisciplinary Association for Psychedelic Studies (MAPS) in 1986 as a one-person nonprofit dedicated to conducting FDA-approved clinical trials of psychedelics and MDMA, eventually growing the organization to nearly 200 full-time staff and shepherding MDMA-assisted psychotherapy through Phase 3 trials for PTSD -- though the FDA ultimately rejected the application in 2024 ([Wikipedia - Rick Doblin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rick_Doblin), [Wikipedia - MAPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multidisciplinary_Association_for_Psychedelic_Studies)). James Fadiman, a Stanford-trained psychologist, is widely credited with popularizing the microdosing movement -- the practice of taking sub-perceptual doses (approximately 10 micrograms of LSD every three days) -- after collecting self-report data from over 1,000 individuals across 59 countries showing improvements in mood, creativity, and focus, though rigorous placebo-controlled trials have produced mixed results ([Mushroom Revival - Fadiman](https://www.mushroomrevival.com/blogs/podcast/microdosing-psilocybin-with-dr-james-fadiman), [PubMed - Microdosing Safety](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30925850/)).

## 6. Remote Viewing

Remote viewing research began in 1972 at the Stanford Research Institute (SRI) in Menlo Park, California, when laser physicist Harold "Hal" Puthoff and physicist Russell Targ tested the psychic claims of artist Ingo Swann, producing results that attracted the attention of the CIA's Directorate of Science and Technology and led to the funding of Project SCANATE ("scan by coordinate"), the first in a series of government-sponsored psychic intelligence programs that would span over two decades and cost approximately $20 million in total ([FAS - Star Gate](https://irp.fas.org/program/collect/stargate.htm), [Puthoff - CIA-Initiated RV](https://www.newdualism.org/papers/H.Puthoff/CIA-Initiated%20Remote%20Viewing%20At%20Stanford%20Research%20Institute.htm)). The program underwent numerous reorganizations and code name changes: GONDOLA WISH (1977, Army ACSI), GRILL FLAME (1978, formalized at Fort Meade under INSCOM), CENTER LANE (1983, redesignated INSCOM project), SUN STREAK (1985, transferred to DIA's Scientific and Technical Intelligence Directorate when Army funding ended), and finally STAR GATE (1991, consolidated under DIA with SAIC as contractor) ([Wikipedia - Stargate Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stargate_Project_(U.S._Army_unit))). Over its full lifespan, approximately 40 personnel served in the program at various times, including about 23 remote viewers, operating from buildings 2560 and 2561 at Fort Meade, Maryland.

Pat Price, a former police commissioner from Burbank, California, produced one of the program's most celebrated successes: given only geographic coordinates for a Soviet site, Price described lying atop a building with a giant gantry crane rolling above him, then produced an extraordinarily detailed drawing of an eight-wheeled gantry crane at what turned out to be the nuclear research facility at Semipalatinsk in Kazakhstan -- the CIA's very first operational remote viewing assignment ([Puthoff - CIA-Initiated RV](https://www.newdualism.org/papers/H.Puthoff/CIA-Initiated%20Remote%20Viewing%20At%20Stanford%20Research%20Institute.htm), [CIAOTEST](https://ciaotest.cc.columbia.edu/olj/sa/sa_jan02srm01.html)). Ingo Swann, credited as the creator of the term "remote viewing" and developer of the Coordinate Remote Viewing (CRV) protocol, produced another legendary result when he described a ring around Jupiter during a remote viewing session conducted before NASA's Pioneer 10 flyby -- Swann himself was initially worried he had accidentally viewed Saturn, but the flyby later confirmed the existence of Jupiter's previously unknown ring system ([Wikipedia - Ingo Swann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ingo_Swann)). Joseph McMoneagle, designated "Remote Viewer No. 1," served as an operational viewer throughout the program's military incarnation and received formal recognition for intelligence contributions, later continuing his work as a research subject at SRI and the Monroe Institute.

In 1995, the CIA commissioned the American Institutes for Research (AIR) to conduct a definitive evaluation of the Star Gate program, appointing statistician Jessica Utts and skeptic psychologist Ray Hyman as the primary reviewers -- specifically chosen because they represented opposing positions on the paranormal question while being considered fair-minded scientists ([FAS - Star Gate Evaluation](https://irp.fas.org/program/collect/stargate.htm), [Wikipedia - Remote Viewing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_viewing)). Their conclusions diverged dramatically: Utts analyzed the ten best experiments from the SRI/SAIC research and concluded that the hit rates were consistently above chance baselines and that, taken in context with other parapsychological research (particularly the ganzfeld experiments), the evidence proved the existence of psychic functioning; Hyman countered that while the newer experiments had eliminated obvious methodological defects, Utts's claim that ESP -- especially precognition -- had been proven was premature and the findings had not been independently replicated ([UCI - Utts Response](https://ics.uci.edu/~jutts/may.pdf), [Wikipedia - Stargate Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stargate_Project_(U.S._Army_unit))). Psychologist David Marks later questioned the impartiality of Utts's appointment, noting she had co-published papers with Edwin May, the physicist who served as the primary researcher on the Stargate program for its later years and who continued parapsychological research after the program's termination. The CIA ultimately terminated the program in 1995, concluding in its public statement that remote viewing had not been proven to work by a psychic mechanism and had not been useful operationally -- though this assessment remains contested by former participants and some researchers to this day.

## 7. Near-Death Experiences (NDEs)

Raymond A. Moody Jr., a philosopher and psychiatrist, effectively founded the modern field of near-death experience research with his 1975 book *Life After Life*, based on interviews with approximately 150 people who had been clinically dead or near death and reported vivid conscious experiences; Moody identified a recurring composite pattern of elements -- leaving the body, passing through a tunnel or darkness, encountering a being of light, experiencing a life review, reaching a boundary or point of no return, and returning with a transformed sense of meaning -- and coined the very term "near-death experience" ([Amazon - Life After Life](https://www.amazon.com/Life-After-Bestselling-Investigation-Experiences/dp/006242890X), [IMHU - Raymond Moody](https://www.imhu.org/people/raymond-a-moody)). Moody's work directly inspired the founding of the International Association for Near-Death Studies (IANDS) in 1978 by researchers John Audette, Bruce Greyson, Kenneth Ring, and Michael Sabom, creating an institutional framework for rigorous scientific investigation of the phenomenon ([Wikipedia - Kenneth Ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Ring)). Kenneth Ring, Professor Emeritus of psychology at the University of Connecticut and IANDS co-founder, conducted the first systematic empirical studies of NDEs, documenting a "core experience" with consistent features across diverse populations and demonstrating in his influential book *Heading Toward Omega* that NDEs produce profound and lasting transformations in attitudes, beliefs, and values -- including dramatically increased belief in an afterlife, reduced fear of death, greater compassion, and enhanced spiritual orientation ([Near-Death.com - Kenneth Ring](https://near-death.com/kenneth-ring/)).

The most rigorous prospective clinical study of NDEs was published by Dutch cardiologist Pim van Lommel and colleagues in *The Lancet* in 2001, examining 344 consecutive cardiac arrest patients across 10 Dutch hospitals: 62 patients (18%) reported some form of NDE, with 41 (12%) describing a core experience; the study controlled for medication, duration of arrest, and other physiological variables and found that none of these factors adequately explained why some patients had NDEs and others did not, challenging purely physiological models ([PubMed - Van Lommel 2001](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11755611/), [Van Lommel - Non-local Consciousness](https://www.newdualism.org/papers/P.vanLommel/Lommel-JCS2013.pdf)). Sam Parnia's AWARE (AWAreness during REsuscitation) study, published in 2014, was the first multi-center investigation to use objective visual targets placed on shelves visible only from above to test whether out-of-body perceptions were veridical; of 2,060 cardiac arrest events, 140 survivors were interviewed, with 9% reporting NDE experiences consistent with awareness and 2% exhibiting full awareness, though only one patient could be tested against the visual targets -- and in that case, none were visible from his location ([PubMed - AWARE I](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25301715/)). AWARE II, published in 2023, expanded the methodology to 25 sites with continuous EEG and cerebral oxygenation monitoring, finding that 39.3% of interviewed survivors reported memories suggestive of consciousness during CPR, and -- most remarkably -- that normal EEG patterns (delta, theta, and alpha activity) consistent with consciousness emerged up to 35-60 minutes into CPR despite severe cerebral ischemia with mean regional cerebral oxygen saturation of only 43% ([PubMed - AWARE II](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37423492/), [NYU Langone News](https://nyulangone.org/news/new-studies-explore-end-life-cognitive-thought-improved-cardiopulmonary-resuscitation-methods)).

Among the most compelling veridical NDE cases are Maria's shoe (a migrant worker who, during a cardiac arrest NDE, described a dark blue tennis shoe with a worn spot over the little toe and a lace tucked under the heel sitting on a third-floor hospital ledge -- social worker Kimberly Clark Sharp investigated and found the shoe exactly as described, visible only from outside the building) and the Pam Reynolds case (a woman who underwent "Operation Standstill" -- hypothermic cardiac arrest with blood drained from her brain, eyes taped shut, and molded speakers emitting clicks in her ear canals -- yet accurately described the surgical saw, its case, specific conversations among medical staff, and details of the procedure observed from a vantage point above the surgeon's shoulder) ([Psi Encyclopedia - Pam Reynolds](https://psi-encyclopedia.spr.ac.uk/articles/pam-reynolds-near-death-experience/), [IANDS - Key Facts](https://iands.org/ndes/about-ndes/key-nde-facts21.html?showall=1)). Neuroscience offers several explanatory models: the DMT release hypothesis (popularized by Strassman, partially supported by the 2019 rat study showing DMT surges during cardiac arrest), cerebral hypoxia and anoxia causing visual cortex disinhibition (producing tunnel-like perceptions), temporal lobe seizure activity (stimulation of the temporal lobe can induce out-of-body experiences and a sense of presence), and REM intrusion -- though none of these models individually accounts for all NDE features, particularly verified veridical perceptions ([PMC - Multidisciplinary Hypothesis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3769617/), [Neuroscience News](https://neurosciencenews.com/near-death-hallucinations-10377/), [PMC - DMT and NDE](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6107838/)). Bruce Greyson, Chester F. Carlson Professor Emeritus of Psychiatry at the University of Virginia and co-founder of IANDS, has dedicated over 50 years to NDE research, studying more than 1,000 cases and producing over 150 publications in academic journals; his 2021 book *After* synthesizes his career's work, arguing that NDEs cannot be fully explained by known brain mechanisms and challenge materialist assumptions about the relationship between consciousness and the brain ([Bruce Greyson](https://www.brucegreyson.com/), [Tim Ferriss - Greyson](https://tim.blog/2024/10/23/near-death-experiences-dr-bruce-greyson/), [UVA - Perceptual Studies](https://med.virginia.edu/perceptual-studies/our-research/near-death-experiences-ndes/)).

## 8. Kundalini

![Kundalini chakra illustration](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/22/Chakras.png)

Kundalini, described in Tantric traditions as a dormant cosmic creative energy coiled like a serpent (kundalini literally means "coiled one" in Sanskrit) at the base of the spine in the muladhara chakra, is a concept that originated in the esoteric practices of Hindu and Buddhist Tantra, where the specific aim of the practitioner is to open the central channel (sushumna nadi) through yogic techniques including pranayama (breath control), bandhas (energy locks), mudras, mantra recitation, and meditation, allowing the kundalini shakti to ascend through the seven major chakras from the base of the spine to the crown of the head (sahasrara), culminating in a state of spiritual liberation or enlightenment ([Yoga Magazine](http://www.yogamag.net/archives/1990s/1994/9405/9405kund.html), [East India Story](https://eastindiastory.com/kundalini-serpent-a-myth/)). The most famous first-person account of kundalini awakening is Gopi Krishna's autobiography, originally published in 1967 as *Kundalini: The Evolutionary Energy in Man* (later expanded and republished as *Living with Kundalini*), in which the Indian civil servant described how, at age 34, after 17 years of daily meditation, he experienced a sudden and violent awakening that sent a stream of liquid light roaring up his spine to his brain, initiating years of physical agony, psychological terror, and intermittent ecstasy as the energy established itself -- an account that provided Western audiences with their first detailed, experiential map of the kundalini process ([Shambhala - Kundalini](https://www.shambhala.com/kundalini-9781645473442.html), [Google Books - Living with Kundalini](https://books.google.com/books/about/Living_with_Kundalini.html?id=BmnXAAAAMAAJ)). Krishna's account catalyzed Western interest in the phenomenon and prompted researchers to investigate whether kundalini experiences had physiological correlates that could be measured and studied scientifically.

Dr. Lee Sannella, a San Francisco psychiatrist and ophthalmologist, conducted pioneering medical research on kundalini in the 1970s, documenting 15 clinical case studies of patients undergoing kundalini awakening in his landmark book *The Kundalini Experience: Psychosis or Transcendence?*, in which he identified what he termed "physio-kundalini syndrome" -- a constellation of measurable physical phenomena including involuntary body movements (kriyas), unusual breathing patterns, sensations of heat or energy flowing along the spine, visual phenomena (inner lights), and auditory experiences (internal sounds or nada) that matched ancient Tantric descriptions with remarkable accuracy ([Earthly Awaken](https://www.earthlyawaken.com/kundalini-syndrome-spiritual-energy-or-nervous-system-crisis/), [Spiritual Crisis Network](https://spiritualcrisisnetwork.uk/kundalini-books)). Sannella argued forcefully that these experiences, while often terrifying and destabilizing, were not psychotic episodes but rather a psychophysiological transformation process with its own internal logic and trajectory. Modern research suggests kundalini activation involves the autonomic nervous system, cerebrospinal fluid dynamics, and subtle electromagnetic phenomena, though mainstream neuroscience has yet to produce a comprehensive explanatory model. The reported symptoms of kundalini awakening include intense heat or cold, spontaneous body movements or postures, emotional volatility ranging from ecstasy to terror, visual and auditory hallucinations, altered states of consciousness, pain along the spine, and disruptions in sleep, appetite, and cognitive function -- symptoms that can persist for months or years and that are frequently misdiagnosed as psychiatric disorders including psychosis, bipolar disorder, or dissociative disorders ([Yogic Counseling](https://www.yogicounseling.com/spiritual-emergency)).

Stanislav Grof, the Czech-born psychiatrist who pioneered research into non-ordinary states of consciousness through LSD-assisted psychotherapy and later developed Holotropic Breathwork, introduced the concept of "spiritual emergency" (a deliberate play on "spiritual emergence") in collaboration with his wife Christina Grof, defining it as a crisis in which the process of spiritual growth and transformation becomes overwhelming, chaotic, and difficult to integrate, potentially mimicking or triggering psychotic symptoms ([Amazon - Spiritual Emergency](https://www.amazon.com/Spiritual-Emergency-Personal-Transformation-Consciousness/dp/0874775388), [CIIS Digital Commons](https://digitalcommons.ciis.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1141&context=ijts-transpersonalstudies)). The Grofs identified kundalini awakening as one of the primary forms of spiritual emergency, alongside shamanic crisis, past-life experiences, psychic opening, and near-death experiences, arguing that if these episodes are properly understood and supported rather than suppressed with medication, they can lead to emotional healing, personality transformation, and expanded consciousness. The American Psychiatric Association eventually acknowledged this dimension by including a diagnostic category for "Religious or Spiritual Problem" (V62.89) in the DSM-IV, though the clinical application of this category remains inconsistent and many patients experiencing spiritual emergencies continue to receive standard psychiatric treatment that may impede the natural resolution of the process ([1drea.com](https://1drea.com/exploring-the-depths-of-spiritual-emergence-kundalini-awakening-a-journey-through-transformation-distinct-from-hppd-and-psychosis/)).

## 9. Meditation Neuroscience

The neuroscientific study of meditation was transformed by Richard Davidson, a neuroscientist at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, whose collaboration with the Dalai Lama (beginning with a 1992 invitation to Dharamsala, India) led to groundbreaking research on the brains of highly experienced Tibetan Buddhist monks who had accumulated between 10,000 and 50,000 hours of meditation practice over periods of 15 to 40 years ([ABC News - Davidson](https://abcnews.go.com/Health/neuroscientist-richie-davidson-dalai-lama-gave-total-wake/story?id=40859233), [Waisman Center](https://www.waisman.wisc.edu/2007/06/25/research-of-richard-davidson-shows-how-meditation-changes-the-mind/), [NIH Record](https://nihrecord.nih.gov/2016/07/01/davidson-lectures-benefits-meditation)). Using EEG, Davidson's team discovered that monks practicing compassion meditation could self-induce sustained, high-amplitude gamma-band oscillatory synchrony at levels approximately 25 times stronger than those seen in novice meditators -- a phenomenon Davidson described as unprecedented in human neuroscience, noting "not a burst of gamma, but a long duration for minutes while they were meditating, which is crazy... this had never been seen in a human brain before" ([Washington Post](http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A43006-2005Jan2.html), [Morphus](https://morphus.io/blog/exploring-the-monk-mind/)). fMRI studies further revealed that expert meditators showed dramatically altered connectivity in the default mode network (the brain's "autopilot" system associated with self-referential thinking and mind-wandering), the salience network, and the sensorimotor network, along with increased alpha-band EEG activity and decreased delta activity during meditation compared to rest ([PMC - Tibetan Monks fMRI-EEG](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11098278/)).

Sara Lazar's neuroimaging research at Massachusetts General Hospital and Harvard Medical School provided the first structural evidence that meditation physically changes the brain: her 2005 study using MRI to measure cortical thickness in 20 experienced Insight (Vipassana) meditation practitioners found that brain regions associated with attention, interoception, and sensory processing -- specifically the prefrontal cortex and right anterior insula -- were significantly thicker in meditators compared to matched controls, and remarkably, this cortical thickening was proportional to the amount of meditation experience ([PMC - Lazar 2005](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1361002/), [Mass General - Lazar Lab](https://www.massgeneral.org/psychiatry/research/lazar-lab-for-meditation-research)). A subsequent 2011 study led by Lazar showed that just eight weeks of Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) produced measurable increases in gray matter density in the hippocampus (associated with learning and memory), the temporo-parietal junction (associated with perspective-taking and empathy), and the cerebellum, along with decreased gray matter in the amygdala (associated with stress and anxiety) -- the first study to document meditation-produced structural brain changes over time in beginners ([Harvard Gazette](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2011/01/eight-weeks-to-a-better-brain/), [PubMed - Gray Matter Density](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21071182/)).

Judson Brewer, a psychiatrist and neuroscientist now at Brown University's Mindfulness Center (formerly at Yale), has conducted pioneering research on how mindfulness training can disrupt addiction and habitual behavior by targeting the brain's reward-based learning system; his randomized controlled trial comparing mindfulness training to the American Lung Association's Freedom From Smoking program found mindfulness to be approximately twice as effective for smoking cessation, with significant differences in both number of cigarettes smoked and abstinence rates at four-month follow-up ([PubMed - Brewer Smoking Cessation](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21723049/), [Wikipedia - Judson Brewer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judson_A._Brewer)). Jon Kabat-Zinn, a molecular biologist and longtime Zen practitioner, developed Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) at the University of Massachusetts Medical School in 1979, creating the foundational secular framework through which meditation entered mainstream Western medicine, psychology, and culture -- MBSR is now offered in over 700 clinics and hospitals worldwide and has been the subject of thousands of research studies ([Meditation4Leadership](https://www.meditation4leadership.org/scientific-resources/)).

However, the field of meditation research has faced significant methodological criticism, most comprehensively articulated in Nicholas Van Dam and colleagues' influential 2018 paper "Mind the Hype" published in *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, which found that much clinical mindfulness research lacks scientific rigor: many intervention studies lack active control groups, there is no consensus definition of "mindfulness" (making operationalization and measurement inherently problematic), self-report mindfulness scales show different factor structures between meditators and non-meditators, fewer than 25% of meditation trials actively monitored for negative or adverse experiences as of 2015, and replication of earlier studies with appropriately randomized designs and proper active control groups remains "absolutely critical" and largely unachieved ([PMC - Mind the Hype](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5758421/), [Psychological Science - Mind the Hype](https://www.psychologicalscience.org/news/releases/mindfulness-and-meditation-need-more-rigorous-study.html)). The authors concluded that "evidence for use of MBIs [mindfulness-based interventions] in clinical contexts should be considered preliminary," a sobering counterpoint to the often breathless media coverage of meditation's benefits.

## 10. Global Consciousness Project

The Global Consciousness Project (GCP) was created in 1998 by Roger Nelson, a research psychologist at the Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) laboratory at Princeton University, building on earlier work with Random Event Generators (REGs) that suggested human consciousness might interact with physical random systems; the project established a network of up to 70 hardware random number generators (also called "eggs") placed at host sites around the world, continuously streaming data to a central server at Princeton, with the hypothesis that major global events eliciting widespread emotion or focused attention might produce statistically detectable deviations from expected randomness in the network's output ([GCP Website](https://noosphere.princeton.edu/), [Wikipedia - GCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Consciousness_Project), [IONS - GCP](https://noetic.org/research/global-consciousness-project/)). The project's methodology involves pre-registering specific world events, defining analysis windows before examining data, and applying statistical tests to determine whether the network's output during these events deviates from chance expectation more than would be predicted by ordinary random fluctuation. An early prototype for the GCP design came in September 1997, when researchers collected continuous data from 12 independent REGs in the US and Europe during the funeral of Princess Diana, finding composite deviations that would occur by chance only once in 100 repetitions of such an experiment ([Psi Encyclopedia - GCP](https://psi-encyclopedia.spr.ac.uk/articles/global-consciousness-project/)).

The September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks produced the GCP's most discussed and contested results, with Nelson and Dean Radin (Chief Scientist at the Institute of Noetic Sciences, which funds the GCP) reporting significant deviations in the network's randomness during and after the attacks ([ScienceDirect - Mass Consciousness](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1550830711002321)). However, independent scientists Edwin May and James Spottiswoode conducted their own analysis of the September 11 data and concluded there was no statistically significant change in randomness during the attacks, arguing that the apparent significant deviation reported by Nelson and Radin existed only within their chosen time window -- a finding that exemplifies the core methodological critique of the project ([Wikipedia - GCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Consciousness_Project), [ResearchGate - Analysis of GCP](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/264461473_AN_ANALYSIS_OF_THE_GLOBAL_CONSCIOUSNESS_PROJECT)). The cumulative results of the GCP's formal experiment, spanning 500 pre-registered events from 1998 to 2015, showed a 7.3-sigma deviation from chance expectation -- corresponding to odds against chance of approximately 3 trillion to one -- a result that physicist Peter Bancel independently verified by downloading all data and replicating the complete analysis ([Medium - Dean Radin Interview](https://medium.com/predict/dean-radin-on-global-consciousness-parapsychology-and-psychic-research-685b4d3b7382), [Medium - Measuring Global Consciousness](https://medium.com/@timventura/measuring-global-consciousness-with-random-number-generators-4d7cc0581da7)).

Dean Radin, who has been deeply involved in the GCP since its inception and is perhaps the most prominent public advocate for the reality of psi phenomena, has presented these results as strong evidence for a collective consciousness that affects physical reality, while noting that the mechanism remains unknown ([IONS - GCP 2.0](https://noetic.org/research/global-consciousness-project-2/)). Skeptics including Robert T. Carroll, Claus Larsen, and others have argued that the GCP's methodology is fundamentally flawed by selection bias and post-hoc pattern matching: there is no objective criterion for determining which events are "significant" enough to warrant analysis, no fixed time frame for how close a data anomaly must be to an event to count as correlated, and random distributions naturally contain spikes and fluctuations that can appear meaningful when selectively examined ([Skepdic - Global Consciousness](https://skepdic.com/globalconsciousness.html)). Perhaps most interestingly, Peter Bancel's own 2017 analysis, while confirming the 7-sigma statistical anomaly, concluded that the data actually "do not support the global consciousness proposal" as originally framed, instead favoring an interpretation involving a "goal-oriented effect" -- suggesting that even if something anomalous is occurring, it may not be what the GCP's proponents believe ([Wikipedia - GCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Consciousness_Project)). A second-generation project, GCP 2.0, has been launched by IONS with updated hardware and methodology to address some of these critiques and continue the investigation.
